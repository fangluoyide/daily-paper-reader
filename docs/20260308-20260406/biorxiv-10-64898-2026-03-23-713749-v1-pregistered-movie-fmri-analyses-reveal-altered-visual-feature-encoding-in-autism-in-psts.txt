Abstract Sensory–perceptual differences are widely reported in autism, yet their underlying 12 

mechanisms remain unclear. We tested preregistered hypotheses using stacked encoding 13 

models applied to naturalistic movie-viewing fMRI from children and adolescents with and 14 

without an autism diagnosis from the Healthy Brain Network. We mapped cortical 15 

responsiveness to low- and high-level auditory and visual feature spaces. Contrary to enhanced 16 

perceptual functioning predictions, autism was not associated with increased low-level encoding 17 

in primary sensory cortices. Instead, autistic children and adolescents had reduced high-level 18 

visual representations and a relative shift toward low-level over high-level feature encoding in 19 

integration and social brain regions including the pSTS and adjacent face/social areas. In pSTS, 20 

this high–low weighting tracked Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS) scores. By contrast, 21 

audio–visual modality preference and sensory dominance were broadly conserved across groups. 22 

Developmentally, encoding exhibited strong, lateralized, modality-congruent age effects. 23 

Together, these findings favor weak central coherence accounts over early sensory enhancement, 24 

constrain mechanisms to altered visual feature weighting within social/multisensory networks, 25 

and demonstrate the value of naturalistic stimuli and encoding models for characterizing 26 

sensory-perceptual neurodevelopmental differences. 27 28 

## Introduction 29 

Sensory processing differences are a common and clinically meaningful feature of autism with a 30 

profound impact on daily life ( Ben-Sasson et al., 2007 ; Chamak et al., 2008 ). As many as 90% of 31 

autistic individuals exhibit altered sensory function ( Leekam et al., 2007 ; Tavassoli et al., 2014 ) with 32

Theories of sensory differences 44 

A number of different theories exist surrounding autistic sensory differences. Enhanced percep-45 

tual functioning (EPF) emphasizes heightened low-level processing and improved discrimination 46 

(Mottron and Burack, 2001 ; Mottron et al., 2006 ), while weak central coherence (WCC) highlights 47 

a local-over-global perceptual preference ( Frith, 1989 ; Happé et al., 2006 ) (see Figure 1 A for an 48 

illustrated comparison). Related Bayesian accounts propose altered use or updating of perceptual 49 

priors or an atypical balance between statistical likelihood and priors ( Pellicano and Burr, 2012 ;50 

Schneebeli et al., 2022 ; Brock, 2012 ) and further, predictive-coding, a process-level instantiation 51 

of these Bayesian ideas, implicates imbalances between prediction errors and top-down predic-52 

tions ( Mumford, 1992 ; Rao and Ballard, 1999 ; Friston, 2005 ; Lawson et al., 2014 ; Van de Cruys 53 

et al., 2014 ; Lawson et al., 2017 ). More mechanistically, neurobiological accounts emphasize exci-54 

tation–inhibition (E/I) imbalances and neural noise, with support from EEG, genetics, and animal 55 

studies ( Rubenstein and Merzenich, 2003 ; Dinstein et al., 2012 ; Puts et al., 2017 ; Wei et al., 2021 ;56 

Hollestein et al., 2023 ; Han et al., 2012 ; Robertson et al., 2016 ). An integrative view links E/I alter-57 

ations to disrupted divisive normalization central to causal inference and predictive coding ( Noel 58 

and Angelaki, 2023 ). In recent years, “Sensory-first” accounts of autism argue that perceptual dif-59 

ferences precede and scaffold downstream social–communicative phenotypes ( Levit-Binnun et al., 60 

2013 ; Cascio et al., 2016 ; Robertson and Baron-Cohen, 2017 ; Falck-Ytter and Bussu, 2023 ; Russo 61 

et al., 2025 ; Takesian and Hensch, 2013 ). 62

Developmental maturation of sensory and association systems 99 

Beyond autism, it is useful to note several robust developmental trends. Primary sensory cortices 100 

mature relatively early, whereas higher-order association regions (e.g., pSTS, TPJ, FFA) show pro-101 

tracted structural–functional specialization into adolescence ( Thompson and Nelson, 2001 ; Sydnor 102 

et al., 2021 ). Multisensory processing also sharpens with age: audiovisual temporal binding win-103 

dows narrow, and cross-modal influences in STS become more reliable and congruent with task 104 

demands ( Neil et al., 2006 ; Burr and Gori, 2012 ). These maturational trends imply that group dif-105 

ferences observed in association cortices and multisensory networks must be interpreted against 106 

a baseline of ongoing developmental tuning. 107 

Neuroimaging evidence for altered sensory processing in autism 108 

Within autism, neuroimaging findings have consistently implicated primary sensory brain regions 109 

(e.g. motion-related differences in visual cortex ( Robertson et al., 2014 ), enlarged population recep-110 

tive fields ( Schwarzkopf et al., 2014 ), and heightened connectivity within primary visual and audi-111 

tory cortices ( Martínez et al., 2020 ; Cerliani et al., 2015 )). Increased trial-to-trial variability of evoked 112 

responses has also been observed across early visual, auditory, and somatosensory cortices ( Din-113 

stein et al., 2012 ). Atypical early auditory discrimination has been further measured through mis-114 

match negativity (MMN), with MMN amplitudes directly linked to sensory over-responsivity and 115 

autistic traits ( Cary et al., 2024 ). During audiovisual integration tasks, autistic participants were 116 

found to exhibit auditory-led phase resets in visual cortex, opposite the visual-led synchroniza-117 

tion typical of neurotypical observers, consistent with altered precision and reliability weighting of 118 

sensory cues and tying primary sensory processing alterations to audiovisual perception ( Ronconi 119 

et al., 2023 ). 120 

Alongside these early sensory processing differences, a growing body of research highlights 121 

significant involvement of higher-order cortical regions. These include but are not limited to the 122 

temporoparietal junction (TPJ; found to have decreased cortical thickness ( You et al., 2024 ) and 123 

altered connectivity to the cerebellum ( Igelström et al., 2017 )), the posterior superior temporal sul-124 

cus (pSTS; found to have reduced activation during biological motion perception ( Koldewyn et al., 125 

2011 ; Federici et al., 2020 ) and altered intrinsic and dynamic functional connectivity ( Alaerts et al., 126 

2015 , 2014 ; Shih et al., 2011 ; Guo et al., 2019 )), and the fusiform face area (FFA; found to have 127 

altered volume and hemispheric asymmetry ( Herbert et al., 2002 ; Floris et al., 2025 ) along with 128 

reduced activation in response to faces ( Pierce et al., 2001 ; Schultz et al., 2003 ; Hadjikhani et al., 129 

2007 ), though some later studies found no difference for familiar faces ( Pierce et al., 2004 ; Had-130 

jikhani et al., 2007 )). 131 

Beyond specific low- and high-level regions with altered connectivity, there have been widespread 132 

reports of more generally altered connectivity across the brain, in the form of under-connectivity 133 

(Damarla et al., 2010 ; Abrams et al., 2013 ; Alaerts et al., 2014 ), over-connectivity ( Rudie and Dapretto, 134 

2013 ; Keown et al., 2013 ), and differing local and global connectivity ( Courchesne and Pierce, 2005 ). 135 

These patterns are thought to be dynamic and changing with age ( Lawrence et al., 2019 ). Some 136 

findings point to an anterior to posterior gradient across the brain of under- to over-connectivity 137 

in autism, perhaps explaining enhanced perceptual performance with increased local connectivity 138 

in early sensory areas and disrupted social behavior with decreased local connectivity in frontal 139

While most studies assess activation or connectivity, fewer examine what or how information is 141

Naturalistic stimuli and autism 143 

Naturalistic stimuli (such as movies and narratives) in neuroimaging studies can improve ecological 144 

validity and compliance while also implicitly including rich sensory, semantic, and social features 145 

(Sonkusare et al., 2019 ; Nastase et al., 2020 ; Vanderwal et al., 2019 ; Redcay and Moraczewski, 146 

2020 ; Haxby et al., 2014 ). In autism, movie paradigms have revealed reduced ISC and ISFC and id-147 

iosyncratic responses (foundational and follow-up work by Hasson et al. 2009 ; Salmi et al. 2013 ;Byrge 148 

et al. 2015 ), links to comprehension and symptomatology (e.g., default-mode/control networks; 149 

Bolton and colleagues ( 2018 ; 2020 )), region- or event-specific effects (TPJ, theory-of-mind; Pantelis 150 

et al. 2015 ; Lyons et al. 2020 ), and cross-national replication of ISFC reductions ( Lin et al., 2025 ). 151 

Deficits in audiovisual synchrony relevant to speech have also been reported ( Wang et al., 2024 ). 152 

Most work to date leverages metrics of brain synchrony, while feature-resolved accounts of what 153 

and where information may be differentially represented in these naturalistic contexts remains 154 

relatively unexplored. 155 

Encoding models 156 

Encoding models enable the direct mapping of stimulus features to brain responses, allowing us 157 

to quantify the representation of information across the brain ( Naselaris et al., 2011 ; Diedrichsen 158 

and Kriegeskorte, 2017 ). Encoding models are readily adaptable to high-dimensional, naturalis-159 

tic features ( Samara et al., 2025 ; Khosla et al., 2021 ; Huth et al., 2016 ; Haxby et al., 2011 ) and 160 

some specific implementations, such as as stacked encoding models ( Lin et al., 2024 ) and banded 161 

regression ( Dupré la Tour et al., 2022 ) enable joint tests across feature spaces. In stacked encod-162 

ing models, instead of relying solely on the predictive performance of separate feature models, the 163 

secondary “stacking” stage yields weights that index the relative contribution of each feature space 164 

to the joint prediction, providing, for example, a direct measure of high- vs. low-level or audio vs. 165 

visual perceptual preferences when these feature classes compete to explain the same responses. 166 

Despite their successful applications in vision and language research, encoding model approaches 167 

are relatively underutilized in autism research relative to ISC based methods, particularly in studies 168 

using naturalistic designs ( Dupré la Tour et al., 2025 ; Kriegeskorte and Douglas, 2019 ). 169

Here, naturalistic movie-viewing fMRI data from children and adolescents with and without autism 171 

is analyzed to test a set of preregistered hypotheses (accessible at osf.io/h92gr and osf.io/47kj6; 172 

summarized in Table 1 ). Feature sets are constructed spanning low-level (e.g., brightness, motion 173 

and loudness) through high-level (faces, bodies, and audio categories such as speech and music), 174 

separately for audio and visual streams and feed these into whole-brain grayordinate-level encod-175 

ing models that quantify the explained variance for each feature class. Primary hypotheses : (i) 176 

Relative to non-autistic peers, autistic participants will show greater encoding of low-level percep-177 

tual features in primary sensory and other perceptual regions (H1.1), with a shift in stacked-weight 178 

preference toward low- over high-level features (H1.2); (ii) autistic auditory and visual cortices will 179 

be more unimodal (H2.1), preferentially encoding modality-congruent information and exhibiting 180 

reduced visual dominance (H2.2). Secondary hypotheses : Encoding-model metrics will covary 181 

with clinical phenotype including autism severity and sensory symptoms (H1.3, H2.3). 182 

Key findings 183 

During naturalistic viewing fMRI, autistic children and adolescents have a relative shift toward low-184 

level over high-level feature encoding in integration and social brain regions (including the pSTS), 185 

with a broadly conserved audio–visual feature balance but robust age-related effects. These find-186 

ings inform sensory theories, argue against large, pervasive shifts in modality dominance, and 187 

demonstrate the utility of naturalistic encoding model methods for the investigation of sensory-188 

perceptual neurodevelopmental differences. 189

Results Summary 327 

Overall, this study finds (i) no evidence for increased low-level encoding specific to primary sensory 328 

cortices in autism; (ii) reduced high-level visual feature representations in social and face brain re-329 

gions (STSvp, STGa, FFC) in autism; (iii) a relative preference towards low-level visual features in 330 

the pSTS in autism, strongest in the left hemisphere and linked to SRS score; and (iv) conserved 331 

audio–visual modality dominance and feature encoding in autism. Further, results show that fea-332 

ture encoding becomes increasingly more modality-congruent with age, with lateralized parcel-333 

specific trajectories, though interactions of age with diagnosis or SRS score are sparse. Post-hoc 334 

whole-brain and ADHD-stratified analyses further identify additional brain regions with significant 335 

encoding differences, including left 8BL and left PoI2. 336 

## Discussion 337 

We asked where and how sensory features are differentially represented in the brain during nat-338 

uralistic viewing in autism. First, we found no evidence for stronger low-level encoding in primary 339 

sensory cortices in autism; instead, differences localized to higher-order association regions, most 340 

notably the pSTS and adjacent frontal parcels. Second, in the autistic group, pSTS showed a prefer-341 

ence for lower-level visual information over high-level visual encoding, with perceptual preference 342 

significantly related to SRS scores. Third, audio–visual modality balance was broadly conserved in 343 

autism, with significant effects predominantly observed developmentally in relation to age rather 344 

than phenotypically across diagnostic groups. 345

Implications for sensory theories 346 

The observed pattern of downstream, visual-specific alterations, with reduced high-level represen-347 

tations in social and integration hubs, fits the theory of weak central coherence (WCC) more readily 348 

than enhanced perceptual functioning (EPF), which predicts increased encoding in early sensory 349 

cortex. At a minimum, high-level global integration differences appear to outweigh any enhance-350 

ments of early visual coding in this dataset and experimental context. A hybrid account remains 351 

plausible, where both theories are true or where subtle early differences cascade to association 352 

regions, for example, but our results do not directly support this. 353 

pSTS, lateral pathway, and social perception 354 

The emergence of the pSTS as a locus of autistic group differences in our results is mechanisti-355 

cally informative. The pSTS is known to support perceptual functions including audiovisual speech 356 

(Okada et al., 2013 ), biological motion ( Sokolov et al., 2012 ), and higher-order social perception 357 

(Lahnakoski et al., 2012 ), with changes observed across development ( Xiao et al., 2016 ). Here, 358 

autistic participants showed decreased high-level visual encoding and a relative preference toward 359 

low-level visual features, with a left-hemisphere predominance for the high-level reduction. This 360 

lateralized pattern sits within the context of the proposed third, lateral visual pathway thought to 361 

be specialized for social perception, and raises the possibility that altered visual components of 362 

socially relevant signals might underlie reported downstream effects, such as reduced audiovisual 363 

speech perception, observed behaviorally in autism. One key detail is that the SRS association was 364 

specific to the perceptual preference measure ( 𝑊 𝐻 −𝑊 𝐿 ) rather than raw performance, pointing to 365 

an altered weighting of visual evidence rather than a uniform gain/loss. Another key observation 366 

is that high-level visual 𝑅 2 itself was reduced in social brain regions (FFC/STS/STG), consistent with 367 

a selective reduction in category-level visual information and not a broader effect that might be 368 

attributable to something like increased noise. 369 

Audio–visual dominance: largely conserved 370 

Contrary to predictions related to behavioral “reverse Colavita” reports, we did not observe group 371 

differences in audio–visual modality preference in sensory cortices. Naturalistic, passive viewing 372 

and ceiling/floor constraints in unimodal cortices may attenuate sensitivity to cross-modal shifts 373 

behaviorally measured under different contexts; moreover, the sensory dominance transitions re-374 

ported in psychophysics may precede our sampled age range or require task demands that are 375 

absent here. An interpretation of these findings is that shifts in sensory modality dominance, at 376 

least at the levels measurable by fMRI-based encoding models, are not a hallmark of autistic per-377 

ception during naturalistic movie viewing. 378 

Developmental effects dominate audio and visual encoding 379 

Age explained more variance than the diagnostic group when considering the stacked audio-visual 380 

encoding models. Specifically, encoding performance increased with age within corresponding 381 

modalities (visual model in visual cortex; audio model in auditory cortex, multisensory, and lan-382 

guage areas), and modality preference also became more congruent with regional specialization. 383 

Effects were often lateralized (e.g., right dorsal-stream visual parcels; left language network), con-384 

sistent with known specializations and a general network-specific reweighting. These results demon-385 

strate the ability of encoding models to pick up broad developmental effects while ruling out these 386 

same factors in relation to diagnosis and phenotype. Notably, most age-related effects appeared 387 

only at looser FD thresholds, and stricter FD thresholds disproportionately excluded younger par-388 

ticipants. This highlights the importance of performing analyses with an awareness and trans-389 

parency around factors such as noise and data quality, as this can be a critical factor that affects 390 

downstream results and inferences. 391

Post-hoc stratification results suggest that visual encoding differences are driven by autistic par-393

Sex-based considerations. 400 

Autism shows sex-linked differences in prevalence, diagnostic presentation, and neurobiology, 401 

with growing evidence for partly distinct neural phenotypes in females and males ( Napolitano 402 

et al., 2022 ). Although sex was included as a covariate, the present study was neither designed nor 403 

powered to adjudicate sex-specific encoding effects. Consequently, the pSTS findings and high– 404 

vs. low-level visual reweighting could be disproportionately driven by males. Future work should 405 

pre-register sex-stratified analyses (and Sex × Diagnosis and Sex × Age interactions), recruit ade-406 

quately powered female cohorts, and incorporate pubertal status as a moderator. Such designs 407 

will clarify whether the observed reductions in high-level visual encoding within social–perceptual 408 

networks reflect a male-typical autistic phenotype, a shared mechanism across sexes, or qualita-409 

tively different pathways in autistic females. 410 

Limitations and Future Work 411 

• Stimulus duration. The combined movie runs were comparatively brief, which limited en-412 

coding performance, constrained the feasible feature space (due to overfitting when increas-413 

ing the number of features), and limited the ability to perform noise-ceiling and within-subject 414 

reliability estimation. We therefore used compact, theoretically robust features with cross-415 

validation and permutation testing. Future work using longer acquisitions (and/or repetitions) 416 

would permit richer models to probe for finer-grained effects. 417 

• MSI-related inferences are limited by modality and design. With only mixed audiovisual 418 

runs, no unimodal (A-only/V-only) acquisitions, and no behavioral MSI measures (e.g., tempo-419 

ral binding window or Colavita task), direct tests of multisensory integration were not possible. 420 

Moreover, many audiovisual interactions unfold at millisecond scales beyond the resolution 421 

of the BOLD signal. We therefore used modality-resolved encoding and variance partition-422 

ing to separate audio and visual contributions within fMRI’s constraints. Future work should 423 

incorporate unimodal runs and MSI tasks, and pair fMRI with EEG/MEG to capture fast cross-424 

modal dynamics. 425 

• Sensory phenotyping. No dedicated sensory-processing scales (e.g., SSP/SPM) were avail-426 

able to map our results directly to sensory phenotypes. Using the scales available in the 427 

dataset with sensory related questions (SRS-2 and SCQ) we aggregated and used a Sensory 428 

Subset Score (SSS), though it was not psychometrically validated. Future work should pair 429 

encoding model results with dedicated sensory-processing scales or validate the SSS. 430 

• Sample composition and generalizability. The dataset included only verbally fluent partici-431 

pants and the autism cohort had a male-skewed sex distribution and high ADHD comorbidity, 432 

limiting generalizability, particularly excluding minimally and non-verbal autistic individuals 433 

and autistic females. We included age, sex, socioeconomic status, and site as confounds in all 434 

analyses, and in exploratory analyses, stratified by ADHD diagnosis, but future work should 435 

balance sex, ADHD, and verbal ability and model comorbidity a priori. 436 

• Preprocessing/parcellation choices and lack of functional localizers. We used MMP parcels 437 

without full MSMAll alignment and without subject-specific functional localizers (e.g., a face 438 

localizer for FFA) as they were not available. This may have introduced some degree of regis-439 

tration imprecision and ROI mis-localization. We did perform MSMSulc cortical surface regis-440

• Eye gaze and attention. Autistic differences in fixation patterns during naturalistic viewing 443

Conclusion 455 

In conclusion, through preregistered stacked encoding model analyses applied to naturalistic movie-456 

viewing fMRI data from the Healthy Brain Network, we find little support for pervasive early sensory 457 

enhancement accounts of autism at the level of feature-resolved cortical representations. Instead, 458 

autism-related differences are concentrated in higher-order integration and social–perceptual re-459 

gions, most notably the pSTS, where high-level visual representations are reduced and feature 460 

weighting shifts toward lower-level visual information in a manner that tracks social symptom 461 

severity. At the same time, audio–visual modality preference and unimodal dominance appear 462 

largely conserved, arguing against large global shifts in sensory dominance during passive natural-463 

istic viewing. Developmental effects were prominent, lateralized, and modality-congruent, under-464 

scoring the need to interpret group differences against ongoing maturation and motion-related 465 

sampling constraints in pediatric datasets. Together, these results support mechanistic interpre-466 

tations related to altered visual feature weighting within social/multisensory networks and demon-467 

strate that naturalistic stimuli coupled with encoding-models can provide a scalable, theory-relevant 468 

framework for characterizing how neurodevelopmental conditions may reshape sensory represen-469 

tations in the brain. 470

Motion-based exclusion criteria in neuroimaging studies of autistic children is an important consid-527

of included subjects. Specifically, subjects that are younger and have higher ADOS and SRS scores, 529

across a sweep of 3 framewise displacement (FD) based functional motion thresholds. Specifically, 532

than 40%, 60%, or 80%. By starting with a more stringent threshold and sweeping this across more 534

Encoding Models 615 

The hypotheses in this study are tested via encoding models, specifically looking at model per-616 

formance, unique variance explained, and model weights. Individual subject grayordinate-wise 617 

stacked regression ( Lin et al., 2024 ) is used, training a separate encoding model for each grayordi-618 

nate to predict withheld brain activation for each subject. Three different stacked encoding models 619 

are employed here: a low- and high-level audio model with only audio features, a low- and high-620 

level visual model with only visual features, and an audio and visual model with all audio and visual 621 

features (the audiovisual stacked model is depicted in Figure 12 ). For each model and subject, the 622 

significance of each grayordinate is tested via a null model by repeatedly temporally permuting 623 

the order of observations and retraining and testing the models. 1,000 iterations are performed 624 

for each subject. FDR correction is applied to account for multiple comparisons for modeling and 625 

testing a separate model for each grayordinate across the brain. Model weights and performance 626 

are interpreted by pooling data across significant grayordinates within parcels of interest. The en-627 

coding models use 5-fold cross-validation, training on 80% of the movie data and testing on the 628 

remaining 20% across successive folds. To account for the hemodynamic response, all features 629 

are convolved with a canonical double gamma HRF function. Features are z-scored to reduce the 630 

effect of scaling on model weights. Accuracy is quantified for each region as the mean coefficient 631 

of determination ( R2) and mean noise ceiling normalized R2. Spearman-Brown corrected split-half 632 

noise ceilings are calculated to estimate the upper bound of possible performance for each brain 633 

parcel. Unique low-level model R2 is calculated by subtracting the high-level model R2 from the 634 

stacked encoding model R2. For this calculation, when high-level R2 is less than zero, it is clipped to 635 

zero, resulting in low-level R2 = stacked R2. When high-level R2 exceeds stacked R2 it is set to stacked 636 

R2, resulting in low-level R2 = 0. Model weight difference is calculated by subtracting the high-level 637 

stacked encoding model weight 𝛼 𝐻 from the low-level weight 𝛼 𝐿 (for the audiovisual model, the 638 

visual weight 𝛼 𝑉 is subtracted from the audio weight 𝛼 𝐴 ) resulting in a value [-1,1] where 1 indicates 639 

a maximum high-level preference, -1 indicates a maximum low-level preference and 0 indicates no 640 

preference between features. 641
