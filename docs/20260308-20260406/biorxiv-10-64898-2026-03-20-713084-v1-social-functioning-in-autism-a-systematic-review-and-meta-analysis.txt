# Abstract 40 

Atypical social functioning is a core feature of autism, yet findings remain fragmented 41 

across components and development. We aimed to systematically integrate this 42 

literature and characterize the organization, development, and moderators of social 43 

functio ning in autism. We conducted a systematic review and meta -analysis of 44 

behavioral studies published between January 1990 and August 2025, identified 45 

through PubMed, Web of Science, and prior reviews, including studies with clinically 46 

diagnosed autistic indi viduals and neurotypical controls. A qualitative synthesis and two 47 

complementary quantitative meta -analyses were performed, with risk of bias evaluated 48 

through study -level characteristics. A total of 2,622 studies (94,114 autistic and 172,847 49 

neurotypical individuals across 32 countries) were included, covering 22 social 50 

components that clustered into five domains. Overall group differences were substantial 51 

(Hedges ’ g = -0.744, 95% CI [ -0.797, -0.690]). Differences emerged earliest in 52 

motivation -based proce sses (~6 months), followed by motor, emotion, and inference 53 

domains, and showed age -related divergence alongside improvement in some skills. 54 

Cross -domain analyses revealed stronger interdependencies in autism and an 55 

organizational pattern most consistent w ith serial relationships among domains. These 56 

findings should be interpreted in light of methodological heterogeneity, underpowered 57 

samples, and uneven cultural representation. Together, the results provide an integrative 58 

framework for understanding the or ganization and development of social functioning 59 

in autism, with implications for precision subtyping, developmentally timed 60 

interventions, and neurodiversity -informed research and policy. This study was pre -61 

registered (PROSPERO: CRD42024566141). 62

# Introduction 66 

Atypical social functioning is a core feature of autism spectrum condition (ASC), 67 

affecting around 1% of the global population and impacting communication, 68 

relationships, and social navigation across the lifespan 1–3. Despite decades of research, 69 

interventions for social difficulties in autism have shown limited real -world efficacy. 70 

While some therapies improve specific social skills in structured settings, these gains 71 

often fail to generalize to everyday interactions , and their long -term sustainability 72 

remains unclear 4,5 .73 

This lack of progress reflects a persistent gap between basic research and clinical 74 

practice —a challenge amplified by the complexity and heterogeneity of autism 6,7 .75 

Basic research often isolates individual aspects of social functioning, adopting a 76 

reductionist approach that overlooks the broader, interdependent nature of social 77 

abilities in autism 8,9 . Clinically, interventions focus primarily on observa ble social 78 

performance —such as verbal communication and nonverbal behavior s—while 79 

underlying cognitive mechanisms that shape these abilities remain underexplored 10 .80 

However, social difficulties in autism are neither static nor isolated but rather part of a 81 

dynamic system of interrelated abilities, spanning basic processes (e.g., social attention ,82 

imitation ) to highe r-order skills (e.g., theory of mind, social communication) 11,12 . Here ,83 

we use the term social functioning to encompass this full range —from foundational 84 

socio -cognitive and motivational processes to observable social behaviors —as all 85 

contribute to how individuals engage with the social world 92 . Despite growing 86 

recognition of these complexities, research remains fragmented and poorly integrated, 87 

making it difficult to translate scientific advances into clinical applications. As a result, 88 

critical questions —which aspects of social functio ning show the greatest divergence 89 

from neurotypical (NT) expectations and what mechanisms drive these differences —90 

remain unresolved, limiting the development of targeted supports tailored to different 91 

social profiles .92 

Critical methodological challenges continue to limit progress in understanding 93

Developmental trajectories add further complexity to the understanding of social 107

social reciprocity , often become increasingly challenging during adolescence and 111

could be most e ffective. However, many existing approaches do not fully account for 114 

developmental change, often applying the same interventions across all ages 6.115 

Understanding how social abilities evolve over time is essential for optimizing 116 

intervention timing and improving long -term outcome s 25,26 .117

Despite these challenges, no integrated synthesis has integrated findings across all 158

complexity of autistic social experience. To address this gap, we ad opted a multi -163 

layered analytic strategy. First, we conducted a qualitative systematic review to map the 164 

breadth and distribution of reported social differences across 22 social components. 165 

Second, we performed a quantitative meta -analysis of group differen ces to estimate the 166

methodological, and sociocultural moderators. Third, recognizing that social abilities 168 

are interdependent rather than isolated, we conducted a quantitative met a-analysis of 169 

behavioral correlations, including meta -analytic structural equation modeling, to 170 

evaluate how different domains of social functioning relate to one another and whether 171 

these relationships conform to theoretically proposed organizational arch itectures. 172 

Together, these complementary analyses provide a structured framework for 173 

understanding how social abilities are organized, develop over time, and are shaped by 174 

contextual factors in autism, with implications for future research, developmentally 175 

timed interventions, and precision -based clinical applications 42,43 .176

## Study Characteristics and Scope 179 

Based on prior theoretical frameworks (see details in Methods section) 44 –47 , a 180 

systematic review identified 2,622 eligible studies published between January 1990 and 181 

August 2025 from PUBMED, Web of Science, and previous meta -analyses. The review 182 

involved data from 94,114 autistic and 172,847 neurotypical individuals across 32 183 

countries. The studies examined 22 key social components 48 (e.g., social attention, 184 

action perception, person perception , empathy, theory of mind, self -processing ,185 

imitation, pretend play, deception , communication , see the complete list in Fig. 1a ). 186 

Definitions of these components are provided in Extended Data Table 1. The PRISMA 187 

flowchart (Fig. 2 ) illustrates the study selection process. As the present review focused 188 

on social difficulties without int ellectual disability , studies on autistic individuals with 189 

co -occurring intellectual disabilities were excluded. Th is meta -analysis was pre -190 

registered on PROSPERO (see details in Methods) .191

## Methodological Scrutiny 211 

Significant methodological variabilit y and biased distributions were observed 212 

across studies. Research efforts were unevenly distributed across social components 213 

(Fig. 1a) , with theory of mind having been extensively studied ( 552 studies), whereas 214 

social learning was notably underrepresented (8 studies). The ages of autistic 215 

participants ranged from 0.5 to 54.6 years (median = 13.41 years), displaying a bimodal 216 

distribution (Extended Data Fig. 2b ). S ex ratios were highly skewed, with a median 217 

male ratio of 0.83 3 (Extended Data Fig. 2c ). Sample size s varied widely, ranging from 218 

3 to 1,905 participants (median = 22) (Extended Data Fig. 2a ). Power analyses 219 

indicated that 69.957% (971/1388) of studies did not meet the minimally required 220 

sample size (Extended Data Table 3), increasing the risk of false -positives and reduced 221 

reproducibility, though underpowered studies did not significantly inf late effect sizes 222 

(p = 0.926 ) (Extended Data Fig. 4).223 

Four major sources of methodological heterogeneity influenced the reported social 224 

differences in autism (Fig. 3a ). First, self -report surveys yielded larger social 225 

differences than experimental tasks ( t = 7.477 , p < .001 ), raising concerns about 226 

subjective biases. This is consistent with prior findings of weak convergence between 227 

survey and experiment 49 . Second, differences in the experimental paradigms used 228 

significantly impacted findings . For example, two task paradigms have been used for 229 

studying social motivation: social seeking —the tendency to preferentially select social 230 

over non -social stimuli in simultaneous -choice paradigms —and social reward, defined 231 

as behavioral enhancement in response to socially rewarding outc omes (e.g., increased 232 

accuracy or speed when a smiling face is used as a reward). We found significant group 233 

differences in social seeking ( Hedges’ g = -0.735, 95% CI [ -1.453, -0.016]) but not in 234 

social reward ( Hedges ’ g = 0.025 , 95% CI [ -0.054, 0.103 ]; t = 7.439 , p < .001), 235 

indicating that while autistic individuals show reduced preference for social stimuli, 236 

they do not differ in reward -driven task performance when the reward is social. This 237 

dissociation is consistent with prior meta -analyses (see direct comparison in 238

## Group -difference meta -analysis 281 

Across all 22 soci al components, 75.21% of studies reported differences in social 282 

functioning in autism, while 24.79% found no significant group differences (Fig. 1) .283 

For a qualitative systematic review , s tudy conclusion s were categorized into five groups :284 

“Impaired” ( 62.55% , indicating poorer performance in autistic individuals), “Different” 285 

(1.26% , distinct but not necessarily better or worse), “Complex” ( 11.29% , varying 286 

group differences depending on conditions), “Intact” (24.79% , comparable 287 

performance t o neurotypical populations), and “Outperformed” ( 0.11% , superior 288 

performance in autistic individuals). A quantitative meta -analysis on effect size 289 

confirmed substantial group differences (Hedges ’ g = -0.744 [ -0.797, -0.690] , p < .001), 290 

with findings remaining stable after excluding small -sample studies ( Supplementary 291 

Fig. 1 ). These results were highly consistent with previous meta -analyses on individual 292 

social components ( Supplementary Fig. 2), reinforcing the validity of our f indings 293 

against the established literature. 294

## Cross -domain correlation meta -analysis 312 

To examine how different domains of social functioning relate to one another, we 313 

conducted the correlation analyses in two stages. First, we performed an exploratory 314 

analysis comparing the strength of cross -domain correlations be tween autistic (ASC) 315 

and neurotypical (NT) groups, asking whether associations among domains differed in 316 

magnitude between groups. Second, we used meta -analytic structural equation 317 

modeling (MASEM) to evaluate whether these correlations were organized acco rding 318 

to alternative theoretically motivated architectures. This two -stage approach allows 319 

group differences in association strength to be distinguished from differences in higher -320 

order organizational structure. 321

This study presents the largest and most systematic synthesis of social functioning 396 

in autism, integrating findings from over 2,500 empirical studies spanning 35 years. 397 

Unlike previous meta -analyses, which focus on single social components in isolation, 398 

our study unc overs the interconnected nature of social difficulties in autism , showing 399 

how 22 social components cluster into five hierarchical domains —motor -based, 400 

emotion -based, inference -based, motivation -based, and complex skills (Fig. 1c) .401

## Selection criteria and analytic al strategy 593 

In line with our conceptual framework, we included studies targeting a broad 594

Twenty -two key social components were identified through an extensive review of 599

comprehensive coverage of relevant components . See Extended Data Table 1 for the 602

## Categorize social difficulties in autism into five domains 691 

Four prominent neurocognitive theories of autism (each citation > 2,000) guided 692

al terations in motor -based components like action perception and imitation 85,86 . The 694

## Meta -Analysis of Behavioral Correlations 742 

To characterize how different domains of social functioning relate to one another, 743 

we conducted the correl ation analyses in two stages, separating the comparison of 744

In the first (exploratory) stage, we compared the strength of cross -domain 749

## Publication Bias Assessment 774 

For each task paradigm with ≥10 studies, we tested for publication bias using an 775 

RVE -adjusted approach. Specifically, we applied Egger’s regression within a robust 776 

variance estimation framework (robumeta), clustering effect sizes by study to a ccount 777 

for within -study dependencies. A significance threshold of p < 0.10 was used for 778 

detecting funnel plot asymmetry. When asymmetry was present, PET -PEESE 779 

corrections were conducted within the same RVE model to obtain adjusted effect size 780 

estimates. Th is method preserves statistical power and precision while properly 781 

accommodating non -independent data structures .782
