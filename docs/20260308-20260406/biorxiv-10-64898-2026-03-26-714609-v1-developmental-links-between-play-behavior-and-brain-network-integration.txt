# Abstract 25 

Play is a fundamental aspect of childhood and plays a crucial role in the development of creativity, 26 yet its neural mechanisms remain poorly understood. We tested the hypothesis that more frequent 27 play is associated with stronger functional integration among the default mode network (DMN), 28 executive control network (CN), and salience network (SAL), as these cortical networks have been 29 implicated in creativity in adults. In a preregistered study of infants and toddlers (Study 1; N = 143, 30 10 months–3 years, 67 boys, Baby Connectome Project), parent-reported play and imitation 31 behaviors increased sharply from 1 to 2 years, and were associated with stronger within-DMN 32 connectivity and DMN-CN coupling, controlling for age, sex, and head motion. In middle 33 childhood (Study 2; N = 108, ages 4–11 years, 52 boys), parent-reported play frequency declined 34 with age, as did cross-network coupling involving SAL. However, children who engaged more 35 frequently in play showed higher DMN-SAL and CN-SAL connectivity. Finally, in a quasi-36 experimental comparison (Study 3; N = 45; ages 4-12 years, 20 boys), children enrolled in a 37 curriculum that includes guided play (Montessori) showed higher DMN-SAL and DMN-CN 38 connectivity than peers in traditional schools, suggesting that pedagogies that center child-led 39 exploration might enable protracted brain network integration. Across these three studies, play was 40 consistently associated with greater integration among DMN, SAL, and CN, a pattern previously 41 linked to creativity in adults. Our findings offer a potential mechanism linking childhood play to 42 later creativity through its role in supporting brain integration during development. 43 44

# Introduction 45 

Play is widely considered to be critical for children’s cognitive development (Gilpin et al., 46 2015; Jaggy et al., 2023; Mireille Smits-van der Nat et al., 2024; Muentener et al., 2018; Stagnitti 47 & Lewis, 2015; White et al., 2021). Children who engage in more play tend to have stronger 48 executive functioning skills (Muentener et al., 2018; White et al., 2021), socioemotional skills 49 (Gilpin et al., 2015; Jaggy et al., 2023; Mireille Smits-van der Nat et al., 2024), and language 50 development (Stagnitti & Lewis, 2015). One of the most consistently documented skills associated 51 with play is creativity (Burgdorf & Panksepp, 2006; Golinkoff et al., 2006; Hoffmann & Russ, 52 2012; Luo et al., 2024; Moore & Russ, 2008; Mullineaux & Dilalla, 2009; Russ 2014; Russ, 2020; 53 Russ et al., 1999; Russ & Wallace, 2012; Siviy, 2016; Wallace & Russ, 2015; Yogman et al., 2018), 54 the ability to generate novel and useful ideas (Runco & Jaeger, 2012; Skene et al., 2022). Play is 55 not only correlated with creativity, it also been shown to causally enhance creativity (Howard-56 Jones et al., 2002; Moreau & Engeset, 2016): Children given the opportunity for free play created 57 more imaginative and colorful drawings than children who received direct instruction (Howard-58 Jones et al., 2002). Why does play enhance creativity? One explanation could be that play creates 59 a more open mindset, changing neural dynamics (Siviy, 2016; Weisberg et al., 2014). Here, we 60 test the hypothesis that repeated engagement in play is associated with the development of neural 61 networks implicated in creativity. 62 Across multiple studies, three large-scale brain networks, the default mode network (DMN), 63 executive control network (CN), and salience network (SAL) have been consistently implicated in 64 creativity in adults (Beaty et al., 2014, 2016, 2018; Hoffmann & Russ, 2012; S. Lee et al., 2021). 65 The DMN is involved in processing everything we cannot sense directly, including the past, the 66 future, and the minds of other people (Buckner et al., 2008; Buckner & Carroll, 2007). # Method 134 This study's design and hypotheses were preregistered; see 135 https://osf.io/42qru/overview?view_only=483cccb179f94ac98b4295df9d1fcfbe. 136 

Participants 137 

The Baby Connectome Project (BCP) dataset (https://nda.nih.gov/edit_collection.html?id=2848) 138 includes 343 infants and 812 scans (Howell et al., 2019). Participants were screened to exclude 139 infants born before 37 weeks of gestation, weighing less than 2000 g, or experiencing significant 140 delivery complications (e.g., neonatal hypoxia, NICU stay > 2 days). Additional exclusions applied 141 to children who were adopted, had a first-degree relative with autism, intellectual disability, 142 schizophrenia, or bipolar disorder, presented with significant medical/genetic conditions affecting 143 development, or had contraindications for MRI. fMRI Image Processing 177 

Preprocessed data were made available as part of the publication process by (Q. Li et al., 178 2024). Preprocessing was implemented using AFNI (v17.0.08) (Cox, 1996) and FSL (v6.0.1) 179 (Jenkinson et al., 2012). Functional volumes were truncated by discarding the first 10 volumes, 180 reoriented, and motion-corrected by alignment to the single-band EPI reference image (SBRef). 181 Distortion correction leveraged AP/PA pairs. Functional data were co-registered with structural 182 images (T1 for those older than 6 months; T2 for those 6 months or younger), then normalized to 183 age-specific templates using ANTs SyN. All scans were then aligned to a common six-month 184 template via pairwise deformation minimization. Functional data were resampled to 2 mm 185 isotropic resolution, spatially smoothed (4 mm FWHM), detrended, and subjected to nuisance 186 regression including 24 motion parameters, white matter, cerebrospinal fluid, global signal, and 187 regressors for volumes with framewise displacement > 0.5 mm. Last, temporal band-pass filtering 188 was applied (0.01–0.1 Hz). 189

Functional connectivity 201 

Functional connectivity was calculated using preprocessed BOLD time series from each 202 participant. Time series were extracted for all 400 Schaefer parcels, and pairwise Pearson 203 correlations were computed between parcels to generate a 400 × 400 connectivity matrix for each 204 participant. These correlation matrices were then Fisher z-transformed to improve normality. To 205 facilitate network-level analyses, the parcel-wise connectivity values were subsequently averaged 206 within each of the seven Yeo functional networks, resulting in mean connectivity estimates per 207 network for each participant. 208 

Clustering coefficient / Participation coefficient 209 

For graph-theoretical analyses, we extracted the preprocessed time series from the same 210 400 Schaefer parcels. Using these data, we computed the clustering coefficient for each parcel, 211 which quantifies the extent to which a node’s neighbors are interconnected, reflecting local 212 network segregation. Participation coefficient was calculated for each parcel to assess the extent 213 to which a node connects across different functional networks. For this calculation, the seven Yeo 214 networks were used as the canonical network assignments, allowing us to quantify between-215 network integration. Graph metrics were computed using weighted, undirected networks derived 216 from the Fisher z-transformed connectivity matrices. Metrics were averaged across parcels within 217 each Yeo network to obtain network-level estimates of clustering and participation coefficients for 218 each participant. 219

# Results 227 

From ages 1 to 2 years, play scores increased sharply and then reached a plateau (Figure 228 2A, R2 = 0.111, P < 0.001). Similarly, within-DMN connectivity increased until around 2 years of 229 age (Figure 2B, R2 = 0.029, PFDR < 0.001). In contrast, within-CN connectivity remained relatively 230 stable throughout infancy (Figure 2C, R2 = 0.012, PFDR = 0.036). Between-network connectivity 231 generally decreased with age (Figure 2D, DMN-CN R2 = 0.100, PFDR < 0.001, Table S1), 232 consistent with increasing functional network differentiation during early development. 233 Controlling for age, sex and head motion, higher play scores were associated with stronger 234 within-DMN and within-CN connectivity, and with more positive DMN-CN coupling (Figure 2E, 235 F: Within Default: R2 = 0.015, PFDR = 0.021, Fig 2G: Within Control: R2 = 0.013, PFDR = 0.021, 236 Fig 2H: Between Default-Control: R2 = 0.013, PFDR = 0.021). # Discussion 277 Play in infancy is positively associated with stronger within- and between-network 278 connectivity of the DMN and CN. Interestingly, during this stage, within-DMN connectivity 279 increases and DMN-CN connectivity decreases through development, consistent with previous 280 reports (Yin et al., 2025). Notably, these normative trends toward increasing network segregation 281 coexist with play-related differences characterized by greater within-network coherence and 282 stronger DMN-CN coupling, indicating that play tracks individual variability in functional 283 integration beyond typical developmental changes. 284 Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. First, the BCP collected brain 285 imaging and play questionnaire at separate timepoints, which limited the temporal precision 286 possible in linking neural and behavioral data. In addition, the ITSEA play questionnaire did not 287 differentiate between types of play. Some of the items captured dramatic role-play and some 288 captured social imitation, which may rely on different neural networks. Another limitation 289

However, resting-state connectivity 291 during sleep is thought to reflect stable, trait-like aspects of functional brain organization, and prior 292 work has demonstrated meaningful individual differences in network architecture under these 293 conditions. Finally, although infants’ limited ability to remain still presents substantial practical 294 challenges, recent advances in imaging technology and motion-correction methods may help 295 address this limitation (Ellis & Turk-Browne, 2018). 296 Together, and consistent with our preregistered hypotheses, Study 1 showed that play 297 increased through infancy and was associated with stronger connectivity within the DMN, as well 298 as greater connectivity between the DMN and CN. 299

# Method 422 

This study was not preregistered. 423 

Participants 424 

The Montessori dataset was previously collected and published by (Duval et al., 2023). 425 Participants were drawn from over 20 schools across Switzerland, representing either Montessori 426 or traditional educational settings. Eligibility required children to be between 4 and 18 years old 427 and to have a consistent educational background (Montessori or traditional for at least three years, 428 or since age 4 for children younger than 7 years old). # Discussion 480 

Children who attended Montessori programs showed higher DMN–SAL and DMN–CN 481 functional connectivity than peers in traditional schools, suggesting that environments which 482 facilitate playful learning may help maintain or enhance functional integrations. # General Discussion 501 

Across three studies spanning infancy to middle childhood, we found converging evidence 502 that play is associated with greater integration both within and between large-scale brain networks. 503 In infancy, as play behavior increases rapidly, more frequent play was associated with stronger 504 within-network connectivity for the DMN and CN, and greater DMN-CN coupling. During 505 childhood, when play behavior declines, more frequent play was associated with preserved 506 integration of SAL with DMN and with CN. Moreover, children in Montessori schools showed 507 stronger connectivity between DMN-CN and DMN-SAL than peers in traditional schools, 508 suggesting that learning environments centering child-led play may support the integration of the 509 brain networks. Together, these findings are consistent with our pre-registered hypothesis that 510
