Abstract 50 

Early childhood is a period of rapid brain maturation and gut microbiome assembly, when 51 

emerging behavioral difficulties can shape later mental health and learning trajectories. 52 

Microbiota –gut –brain communication has been implicated in neurodevelopment through 53 

microbial metabolites and immune signaling. However, most pediatric evidence comes 54 

from high -risk or clinically referred cohorts, and gut microbiome –related correlates of 55 

typical behavioral variation in community -based preschool children remain poorly defined. 56 

In a cross -sectional sample of typically developing Japanese preschool children, here we 57 

show that behavioral variation within normative ranges is associated with distinct 58 

microbiome configurations: internalizing domains cluster with signatures consistent with 59 

higher inflammatory potential and elevated nucleotide biosynthesis, whereas somatic 60 

complaints and withdrawn behavior associate with reduced respiratory and fermentative 61 

activity. Sleep -related difficulties show the broadest predicted functional footprint, 62 

including enrichment of pathways related to methyl -donor and heme biosynthesis, while 63 

externalizing domains associate with pathways involved in cell -envelope and carbohydrate 64 

remodeling. In contrast, age, height, and weight track canonical maturation of  gut 65 

microbiome  composition, indicating that behavioral associations are not simple proxies of 66 

growth. Together, these findings extend early -life microbiome research by resolving 67 

domain -specific associations in a low -risk Asian community sample and highlighting 68 

pathway -level candidates that may interface with neurodevelopment. 69

Introduction 74 

Preschool mental health problems are common and impairing rather than transient. Large 75 

epidemiological cohorts and a meta -analysis suggest that about 15 –20% of children aged 1 –76 

7 years meet criteria for at least one mental disorder  [39] . Symptoms cluster into 77 

internalizing, externalizing, and sleep or regulation problems, with sufficient consistency 78 

that preschool adaptations of standard diagnostic nosologies have been proposed  [10] .79 

Longitudinal studies show that distinct internalizing, externalizing, and co -occurring 80 

profiles are detectable by 18 months to 3 years; persistent co -occurring problems predict 81 

the greatest difficulties at school entry; and early symptom clusters, such as sleep problems 82 

at 18 months, predict increased internalizing and externalizing problems at 5 years  [1, 32] .83 

Standardi zed parent -report instruments are therefore central in research and practice. The 84 

Child Behavior Checklist for Ages 1½ –5 (CBCL 1½ –5) is widely used, provides 85 

empirically derived syndrome scales spanning internalizing, externalizing, and sleep -related 86 

problems, and shows robust factor structure and construct validity in population -based and 87 

clinical samples, including preschool children with autism spectrum disorder  [1, 26] . Its use 88 

in longitudinal cohorts that track trajectories from toddlerhood into later childhood enables 89 

internationally comparable phenotyping in the present study  [1, 26] .90 

Among candidate biological markers, the gut microbiota and the microbiota –gut –brain axis 91 

have gained prominence. Intestinal microbes shape host metabolism, immune activation, 92 

and neuroendocrine signaling, and communicate with the brain via short -chain fatty acids, 93 

bile acids, tryptophan metabolites, cytokines, and vagal and enteric pathways  [22] . In 94 

animal models, germ -free rearing, early antibiotic exposure, and colonization with defined 95

Methods 153 

Ethics approval and consent to participate 154 

The study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Education, Mie 155 

University (approval No. 2023 -02). All methods were performed in accordance with the 156 

relevant guidelines and regulations and in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. 157 

Written informed consent was obtained from the parents or legal guardians of all 158 

participating children. 159

Participants and stool collection 161 

To examine associations between gut microbiota, physical growth, and behavioral 162 

development in early childhood, we collected stool samples from 36 preschool children 163 

living in Mie, Okayama and Shiga Prefectures. Guardians received a stool collection kit for 164 

gut microbiota analysis (TechnoSuruga Laboratory, Shizuoka, Japan) and were instructed 165 

to collect a stool sample at home and mail it to a chemistry laboratory at the Faculty of 166 

Education, Mie University. Upon arrival at the laboratory, stool samples were immediately 167 

frozen and stored at  –20 °C until DNA extraction. 168

Achenbach System of Empirically Based Assessment (ASEBA). The CBCL 1½ –5173 

comprises 100 items that are rated 0 (“Not true”), 1 (“Somewhat or sometimes true”), or 2 174 

(“Very true or often true”) and are grouped into seven empirically derived syndrome scales: 175 

Emotionally Reactive, Anxious/Depressed, Withdrawn, Somatic Complaints, Attention 176 

Problems, Aggressive Behavior, and Sleep Problems. In this study, we used the Japanese 177 

version of the CBCL 1½ –5, which has demonstrated adequate reliability and validity  [12] .178 

A detailed list of questionnaire items is provided in Table S1. Item scores were summed to 179 

yield raw scores for each syndrome and the broadband Internalizing, Externalizing, and 180 

Total Problems scales. Raw syndrome scores were used both to describe normative, 181 

borderline, and clinical ranges and as continuous variables in correlation analyses, in line 182 

with Japanese CBCL 1½ –5 manuals. 183

Microbiome profiling and statistical analysis 185 

Stool DNA was extracted using the NucleoSpin ® DNA Stool kit (Macherey‑Nagel, Düren, 186 

Germany) with bead‑beating. The V3 –V4 region of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene was 187 

amplified and sequenced on an Illumina MiSeq platform using paired -end 2 × 300 bp reads. 188 

Reads were processed in  QIIME 2  (v2024.2) with DADA2 to generate amplicon sequence 189 

variants (ASVs), and taxonomy was assigned against the EzBioCloud 16S database. 190 

Functional profiles were predicted from ASVs using PICRUSt2 (v2.5.2) to obtain MetaCyc 191 

pathway abundances. Associations between anthropometrics, CBCL syndrome scores, and 192 

microbiome features (α -diversity indices, genus -level relative abundance, and predicted 193

Results 197 

Physical characteristics and behavioral domains measured by CBCL 1½ –5198 

A total of 36 children completed the CBCL 1½ –5 (Fig. 1, Table S2). Twenty were boys and 199 

sixteen were girls. The mean age was 48.0 ± 8.2 months. Mean body size was 99.0 ± 5.2 200 

cm in height (range 86.5 –112.0 cm) and 15.6 ± 1.8 kg in weight (range 12.5 –21.0 kg). Most 201 

children scored within the normative range across CBCL domains. Borderline elevations 202 

were most common for Internalizing Problems (7/36, 19.4%) and Sleep Problems (5/36, 203 

13.9%). Clinically significant scores were observed most frequently for Externalizing 204 

Problems (6/36, 16.7%), followed by Total Problems (4/36, 11.1%).  No participant met the 205 

clinical threshold for Anxious/Depressed, Withdrawn, or Attention Problems (Table S2). 206 

We used Spearman’s rank correlations to examine associations among age, height, weight, 207 

and CBCL domains (Fig. 2). Height and weight were strongly correlated (ρ = 0.86, p < 208 

0.001). Age correlated positively with height (ρ = 0.60, p <  0.001) and with weight (ρ = 209 

0.48, p =  0.003). Aggressive Behavior correlated with Emotionally Reactive  (ρ = 0.59, p < 210 

0.001)  and with Anxious/Depressed  (ρ = 0.55, p =  0.001) , suggesting co -occurrence of 211 

aggression, emotional dysregulation, and internalizing symptoms. Emotionally Reactive 212 

correlated with Anxious/Depressed  (ρ = 0.62, p <  0.001) . Anxious/Depressed also 213 

correlated with Attention Problems  (ρ = 0.46, p =  0.005)  and Sleep Problems  (ρ = 0.38, p = 214 

0.023) , suggesting shared underlying mechanisms among  internalizing, attentional, and 215 

sleep domains. Withdrawn correlated with Attention Problems  (ρ = 0.44, p =  0.007)  and 216 

Aggressive Behavior  (ρ = 0.34, p =  0.040) , indicating that social withdrawal could co -occur 217

Discussion 395 

Gut microbiota along physical growth trajectories 396 

Age, height, and weight were strongly intercorrelated, but their correlations with CBCL 397 

domain scores were modest (Fig. 2), suggesting that behavioral variation was not a simple 398 

proxy for somatic development. Somatic indices nevertheless tracked canonical microbiota 399 

maturation and predicted functions. The decline of Escherichia and other facultative 400 

anaerobes with age, together with increasing representation of strictly anaerobic genera, 401 

mirrors cohort data describing the transition toward a more stable, adult -like microbiota 402 

during the preschool years  [23] . Age -related enrichment of leucine degradation and 403 

adenosylcobalamin biosynthesis suggests increasing microbial contributions to branched -404 

chain amino -acid catabolism and vitamin B 12  availability as diets diversify. Height and 405 

weight were positively associated with arginine biosynthesis and inversely associated with 406 

succinate -to -butanoate fermentation, pointing to a shift away from reductive succinate 407 

pathways in larger children. The weak coupling between growth indices and CBCL traits 408 

supports the interpretation that the domain -specific signatures below are unlikely to be 409 

simple proxies of maturation. 410 

Overview of microbiota –behavior associations in a community sample 411 

In this cross -sectional study of typically developing Japanese preschool children, gut 412 

microbial community structure and predicted functions showed coordinated associations 413 

with multiple CBCL 1½ –5 domains. Even within normative ranges, variation in 414 

internalizing, externalizing, and sleep -related difficulties aligned with distinct taxonomic 415

Methodological considerations and future directions 485 

These findings should be interpreted as associative rather than causal. The modest sample 486 

size limits statistical power and may reduce generalizability beyond Japanese community -487 

dwelling preschool children, and the cross -sectional design cannot determine whether 488 

microbiome differences precede behavioral traits, reflect shared environments, or arise 489 

through bidirectional feedback. Larger longitudinal cohorts with repeated sampling and 490 

direct multi -omic measurements will be required to test directionality and mechanism. 491 

Across domains, signals converged on microbial capacities related to nucleotide synthesis, 492 

amino -acid and methyl -group metabolism, bile -acid and glycan transformation, and 493 

heptose -containing lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis. These pathways plausibly interface 494 

with neurodevelopment by shaping metabolite availability, epithelial and immune tone, and 495 

neuroendocrine signaling relevant to emotional regulation, attention, and sleep. Future 496 

work that combines repeated stool sampling with detailed diet and medication histories and 497 

concurrent measurement of relevant metabolites and host markers can test whether 498 

microbial methyl -donor metabolism, bile -acid remodeling, and glycan utilization mediate 499 

changes in sleep and behavior over time. If replicated, these signatures could support 500 

microbiome -informed prevention studies in early childhood, including pragmatic trials of 501 

dietary modulation and targeted prebiotic or probiotic approaches, with outcomes anchored 502 

to standardized behavioral scales and objective sleep measures. Over the longer term, 503
