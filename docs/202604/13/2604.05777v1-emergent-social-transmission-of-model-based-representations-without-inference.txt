Abstract 

How do people acquire rich, flexible knowledge about their environment from others despite limited cognitive capacity? Humans are often thought to rely on computationally costly mentalizing, such as inferring others’ beliefs. In contrast, cul-tural evolution emphasizes that behavioral transmission can be supported by simple social cues. Using reinforcement learn-ing simulations, we show how minimal social learning can indirectly transmit higher-level representations. We simulate a na¨ ıve agent searching for rewards in a reconfigurable en-vironment, learning either alone or by observing an expert— crucially, without inferring mental states. Instead, the learner heuristically selects actions or boosts value representations based on observed actions. Our results demonstrate that these cues bias the learner’s experience, causing its representation to converge toward the expert’s. Model-based learners benefit most from social exposure, showing faster learning and more expert-like representations. These findings show how cultural transmission can arise from simple, non-mentalizing processes exploiting asocial learning mechanisms. 

Keywords: Interactive behavior; Social cognition; Agent-based Modeling; Computational Modeling 

# Introduction 

Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a powerful framework for understanding how agents acquire knowledge through inter-action with their environment (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Tra-ditionally, this framework has focused on solitary learning: individual agents learn by interacting directly with their en-vironment and update internal representations based on indi-vidual experience. While this paradigm has yielded substan-tial theoretical and practical advances (Shteingart & Loewen-stein, 2014; O’Doherty, Lee, & McNamee, 2015), it stands in contrast to how learning often unfolds in natural settings: humans and other animals routinely benefit from social infor-mation (Laland, 2004; Olsson, Knapska, & Lindstr¨ om, 2020), leveraging the experiences of others to accelerate learning (Rendell, Boyd, et al., 2010; Park, Go¨ ıame, O’Connor, & Dreher, 2017), reduce exploration costs (Witt, Toyokawa, Lala, Gaissmaier, & Wu, 2024; Garg, Kello, & Smaldino, 2022), and improve performance in complex environments (Wu et al., 2025; Hawkins et al., 2023). Thus, recent work in RL has increasingly incorporated so-cial learning mechanisms, motivated by both the goal of mod-eling human social cognition (Wu, V´ elez, & Cushman, 2022; Hackel, Mende-Siedlecki, & Amodio, 2020; Najar, Bonnet, Bahrami, & Palminteri, 2020) and for engineering applica-tions where artificial agents learn from observing human be-havior (Peters & Schaal, 2008; Urain et al., 2025). In these approaches, learning from others is often modeled as men-talizing (Baker, Saxe, & Tenenbaum, 2009; Jara-Ettinger, 2019)—inferring latent mental states such as goals, prefer-ences, or beliefs about the structure of the environment from observed actions (also known as Theory of Mind; Tomasello, 1999). Techniques like inverse reinforcement learning (IRL; Russell, 1998; Jara-Ettinger, 2019; Jern, Lucas, & Kemp, 2017; Collette, Pauli, Bossaerts, & O’Doherty, 2017) formal-ize this process by inverting the RL model to “unpack” behav-ior into underlying value or model-based representations of the world (i.e., transition structure). Although powerful, such inference methods are computationally costly and intractable in many settings (Wu et al., 2022). In contrast, cultural evolution research has long empha-sized that surprisingly sophisticated patterns of behavioral transmission can emerge from simple, low-cost social cues (Heyes, 2018; Mesoudi, 2016). Individuals often rely on simple heuristics, such as selectively imitating the most suc-cessful (McElreath et al., 2008) or the most prestigious mem-bers of a group (Jim´ enez & Mesoudi, 2019; Boyd & Rich-erson, 1988), without needing to infer mental states of oth-ers. These mechanisms can nonetheless support the accumu-lation of complex behaviors and knowledge across genera-tions (Legare & Nielsen, 2015; Rendell, Fogarty, & Laland, 2010), suggesting effective social learning may not require expensive inferences required for mentalizing (Roberts-Gaal, Bolic, & Cushman, 2025; Uchiyama, Tennie, & Wu, 2023). In this work, we explore how higher-level representations of value and model-based transition structure can emerge in RL agents from simple forms of social learning—without ex-plicit inference of others’ mental states—and how they sup-port generalization to novel conditions. We simulate agents that search for rewards in a spatially reconfigurable environ-ment, learning either independently or by observing an ex-pert. Crucially, learners do not attempt to infer the expert’s goals or internal model of the environment; instead, they rely on social cues to guide learning, adopting strategies more similar to imitation (Byrne & Russon, 1998) or local enhance-ment (Galef, 1988) than mentalizing. # Methods 

We simulated agent behavior across three experiments ( n =

1, 000 per experiment) using a spatially reconfigurable envi-ronment, resembling a foraging-themed board game. Agents navigated generated grid-worlds over 20 episodes to acquire rewards, learning through a combination of individual expe-rience and observing an expert during training (episodes 1-10 only). Agent performance was then evaluated in the test phase (episodes 11-20) to assess generalization in the absence of the expert. 

Environment and Rewards. The environment was com-posed of four quadrants, each forming a 5 ×5 grid with a fixed configuration of walls blocking movement between adjacent states and a single designated reward state (Fig. 1a). In each simulation, we randomly rotated and rearranged these quad-rants to create a 10 × 10 grid world (4! · 44 = 6, 144 unique configurations), generating different global layouts (Fig. 1b). Reward values for each designated reward state were ran-domly assigned (without replacement) at the start of each simulation, based on four distinct reward values: 0 (no re-ward), 25 (low), 50 (medium), and 75 (high). The learner agent had no prior knowledge of either the locations or the values of the rewards. To introduce stochasticity, random noise ε was added for each reward observation defined us-ing a shifted binomial distribution ε ∼ Binomial (n = 400 , p =

0.5) − 200, which yields integer values with a mean of 0 and a variance of 100. This noise was applied independently to each reward in every episode. 

Procedure. At the start of each episode, the agent is placed randomly in one of four start states at the center of the board. The agent moves between adjacent states by executing ac-tions (up, down, left, right) and is constrained by walls: at-tempts to move into a wall result in the agent remaining in the current state. Actions that do not yield a positive reward incurs a cost (negative reward) of −1. An episode terminates either when a positive reward is obtained or after 40 actions. Experiments. We conducted three experiments to assess the learners’ ability to generalize knowledge acquired during training (Fig. 1c). Exp. 1 establishes baseline performance and examines the transfer of values and model-based beliefs from the expert to social learners, without any changes to the environment. In Exps. 2 and 3, the test phase introduced ad-ditional modifications to the task to assess the learner’s ro-bustness to changes in the environment. Exp. 2 modified the reward structure, by swapping the values of two randomly se-lected reward states. Exp. 3 modified the starting locations, by shifting start states one tile towards the corners (excluding reward states), while keeping the spatial layout fixed. 

Computational models 

We compare six computational learning models (Fig. 2) within a 2 × 3 factorial design that contrasts model-free and model-based RL across three forms of social information in-tegration: asocial learning (AS), decision biasing (DB), and value shaping (VS). Asocial learners exclusively rely on indi-vidual experiences, whereas social learners additionally in-corporate expert information either at the action selection level by biasing their policy toward the expert’s behavior (DB), or at the value learning level by directly shaping value representations (VS) based on observed expert actions.

Value shaping (VS) is inspired by theoretical accounts of the local enhancement of stimuli based on observing others engaging with them (Spence, 1937; Galef, 1988). Follow-ing Najar et al. (2020), we implement VS by augmenting the learner’s value representations of actions performed by the expert in a given state:

Eq. 2), increasing the likelihood of ac-tions observed in the expert in the corresponding state. Once the expert reaches a termination step, the VS learner reduces to asocial learning for the remainder of the episode. 

Parameter optimization. We optimized each model’s hy-perparameters using the differential evolution algorithm (Storn & Price, 1997) to maximize performance (cumulative reward) across 1,000 independent simulations in the base-line environment. Optimization for social learners was based solely on the 10 training episodes, whereas asocial agents and the expert were optimized using all available episodes (20 and 120, respectively). Learning rates α and η for social learners were fixed to the values optimized for asocial learn-ers, due to joint optimization consistently converging to low learning rates that suppressed individual learning and led to poor test performance. Candidate parameters were optimized in a transformed, unbounded space to enforce valid ranges (e.g., log-transform for β ∈ [0, ∞]) and a fixed random seed was used to ensure reproducibility. 

# Results 

We first evaluated the performance of all learning strate-gies across episodes, distinguishing between training and test phases to characterize how effectively agents acquire and ap-ply knowledge, depending on their learning strategy. Next, we examined how information is indirectly transferred from expert to social learners by analyzing value and belief rep-resentations, assessing whether social learners acquire sim-ilar value and state transition representations as encoded by the expert. Finally, we tested the robustness of the learned knowledge under environmental changes: in Experiment 2 we assessed how well learners generalize to modified reward configurations, and in Experiment 3 we modified the start-ing position to evaluate whether the belief transferred from the expert is preserved and continues to support behavior un-der novel start conditions. Across analyses, variability across simulations was quantified using the standard error of the mean (SEM). 

Exp. 1: Value and belief transfer without inference 

Exp. 1 establishes a baseline, where the only change from training to test phases is the absence of the expert. We quan-tified performance as the mean cumulative reward per learner across simulations, demonstrating how effectively different learning strategies enable agents to navigate the environment and collect rewards over the course of the 20 episodes. Starting with the asocial agents (AS), we see a strong ad-vantage of model-based (MB) compared to model-free (MF) learning (Fig. 3a). However, all social learning agents (DB and VS) outperformed the best asocial agent (AS-MB) in the training phase as a result of leveraging social information from the expert. During training, VS agents performed bet-ter than DB agents, with no difference between MB vs. MF. However, we see larger differences in the test phase when the expert disappears. The most striking difference is the collapse of DB-MF performance, which falls below the AS-MF agent. By contrast, the DB-MB agent maintains strong performance and, notably, outperforms the AS-MB agent in the test phase, reaching the level of the VS agents (both MB and MF). These results demonstrate the brittleness of policy-based social learning (DB) using only model-free methods (MF), while also showcasing the robustness of model-based social learning (MB). We then computed indirect value transfer based on the cor-respondence between the learner’s and expert’s state-action value representations at the end of each simulation (averaged across simulations). Specifically, we computed Spearman correlations between value functions across states, grouped by distance from the nearest reward to account for goal-relevance (i.e., states closer to rewards have greater rele-vance). This allows us to assess how effectively the learner reconstructs the expert’s value information. Figure 3b shows that except for DB-MF, all social agents achieved greater value transfer compared to asocial baselines (AS-MB and AS-MF). While asocial agents are bound to acquire similar value representations as the expert due to interacting with the same environment, social agents (except for DB-MF) achieve a substantial boost in this respect, providing a mechanistic explanation for their better performance. This value trans-fer effect (excepting DB-MF), displayed goal-relevance, with stronger transfer for states closer to rewards. Lastly, we quantified indirect belief transfer for model-based learners as the correspondence between the learner’s and expert’s transition matrices at the end of each simulation. Exp. 2: Robustness to changes in reward 

In Exp. 2, the test phase additionally included swapping the values of two randomly selected reward states (Fig. 1c). As in Exp. 1, we quantified performance as the mean cumulative re-ward per learner across simulations to assess how effectively different learning strategies generalize to the altered reward contingencies, focusing on the test phase since the training phase was identical to Exp. 1. Performance initially dropped for all agents in the test phase, except for AS-MF, reflecting the challenge of adapt-ing to new reward contingencies. The most pronounced drop occurred for DB-MF agents, reinforcing the vulnerability of policy-based, model-free social learning. # Discussion 

We investigated how rich, flexible knowledge can be socially transmitted without mentalizing, thus sidestepping compu-tationally costly inferences about others’ goals or beliefs. Using simulations with both model-free (MF) and model-based (MB) reinforcement learning (RL) agents, we show that learners can acquire higher-level representations through simple social heuristics. Specifically, decision-biasing (DB) implemented at the policy-level simply seeks to minimize dis-tance to an expert demonstrator (Eqs. 4-5), while value shap-ing (VS) adds a value bonus to state-action pairs observed in the expert, analogous to local enhancement (Eq. 6). Using three simulation-based experiments in a spatial for-aging task, we found that social learning particularly benefits MB agents, which acquire more accurate and robust repre-sentations, while also generalizing better to changes in re-wards and starting locations. Across both MB and MF agents, value-based social learning using VS generally outperformed policy-based methods using DB, echoing a general principle in RL that value is a more generalizable representation than actions (Lehnert, Littman, & Frank, 2020; Wu et al., 2022). What are the mechanisms behind these results? Even sim-ple social heuristics bias the learner’s experience, which, when leveraged by asocial model-based planning, can sup-port more accurate representations of value and environment structure (Wu et al., 2022; Uchiyama et al., 2023). In our simulations, MB agents use Dyna (Alg. 1) to simulate expe-riences and propagate values, analogous to the role of hip-pocampal replay ( ´ Olafsd´ ottir, Bush, & Barry, 2018; Miller, Botvinick, & Brody, 2017; Vikbladh et al., 2019). This sug-gests that insight can emerge from na¨ ıve imitation (Lyons, Young, & Keil, 2007) by exploiting internal planning mecha-nisms, consistent with neural evidence that social observation engages replay-related activity, highlighting a shared neural basis for social and asocial model-based learning. Our findings also offer a perspective on cross-species dif-ferences between humans and non-human primates (NHP; Bandini & Tennie, 2023; Call, Carpenter, & Tomasello, 2005). Humans over-imitate, copying even irrelevant ac-tions (Horner & Whiten, 2005; Lyons et al., 2007), whereas chimpanzees tend to emulate outcomes rather than copy spe-cific behavior (Tennie, Call, & Tomasello, 2010). Our re-sults demonstrate that policy-based social learning (i.e., at the level of actions) is fragile under model-free learning (DB-MF), but robust when paired with model-based mechanisms (DB-MB). While the literature on model-based learning in NHPs is scarce (but see Sato, Sakai, & Hirata, 2023, for a task disincentivizing MB learning), differences in reliance on model-based planning may shift the cost-benefit trade-off between policy-based and value-based social learning (Wu et al., 2022), helping to explain why over-imitation is more adaptive in humans than in chimpanzees. Despite promising results, there are several limitations that could be addressed in future work. First, while we eval-uated robustness to changes in rewards and starting loca-tions, we did not manipulate the underlying environmen-tal structure itself (e.g., changes to transition dynamics or wall layouts), which would allow us to better assess whether non-mentalizing social learning also supports the transfer of deeper structural knowledge. Second, we modeled the ex-pert as a passive demonstrator rather than providing peda-gogical demonstrations (V´ elez, Chen, Burke, Cushman, & Gershman, 2023). Future work could test how such demon-strations might interact with or amplify our minimal social learning mechanisms. Finally, our results are based on sim-ulations, whereas testing these predictions in human behav-ioral experiments will be essential to assess whether similar non-mentalizing processes can also support higher-order so-cial transmission in people. In sum, we provide a computational account of how the indirect transmission of high-level representations can arise from minimal, non-mentalizing forms of social learning that exploit asocial model-based RL mechanisms.
