## Abstract 21 22 The role of digital media in early childhood development remains highly debated, particularly 23 regarding its impact on language acquisition. While excessive or unsupervised screen exposure 24 has been linked to poorer outcomes, less is known about whether structured and interactive uses of 25 technology can support learning. Building on previous research, we evaluated a brief, educator-26 supervised tablet-based intervention in 246 children aged 2-5 years from low- to middle-27 socioeconomic backgrounds attending public early education centers. Using a pre-post design with 28 matched study and control groups, children completed 4-8 short training sessions (15 minutes 29 each) involving interactive word-image associations spanning multiple linguistic categories. 30 Preschoolers additionally engaged in prompted vocalization. 31 Across age groups (2-3, 3-4, and 4-5 years), children in the intervention showed greater gains in 32 language comprehension than controls, including receptive language in toddlers ( β = 0.49, p = 33 0.009), vocabulary and morphology in younger preschoolers ( β = 0.59-0.68, all p < 0.05), and 34 grammar comprehension in older preschoolers ( β = 0.30, p = 0.038). These effects were consistent 35 after accounting for child and parental characteristics. 36 Together, these findings suggest that the developmental impact of digital media depends less on 37 exposure itself than on how it is used. When embedded in structured, socially guided interactions, 38 even brief tablet-based activities may support early language development 39 40

## Introduction 42 43 Despite the widespread use of interactive technologies by young children, clear guidelines on how 44 to maximize their educational benefits while minimizing potential harm remain limited [1]. Concerns 45 about early screen exposure have been widely discussed, particularly in relation to language 46 development and broader neurodevelopmental outcomes. While some studies report associations 47 between high levels of screen time and adverse developmental outcomes, including language 48 delays [2,3], the evidence remains mixed, and causal relationships are difficult to establish due to 49 confounding environmental and familial factors [4,5]. 50 A key distinction emerging from literature is between passive and interactive forms of screen use. 51 Passive exposure, particularly when it displaces caregiver-child interaction, has been associated 52 with reduced verbal engagement and poorer language outcomes in early childhood [2]. In contrast, 53 interactive and socially scaffolded uses of digital media, especially those involving contingent 54 feedback or adult participation, appear to mitigate these risks and may even support aspects of 55 cognitive and language development [6,7]. 56 From a developmental perspective, early language acquisition is highly sensitive to the quality of 57 environmental input, including the richness of linguistic interactions and opportunities for contingent 58 learning [8,9]. Digital tools that incorporate features such as immediate feedback, multimodal input, 59 and guided attention may therefore provide structured opportunities to support language learning, 60 particularly in contexts where access to enriched linguistic environments is limited. This is especially 61 relevant in low- to middle-income settings, where scalable and cost-effective interventions are 62 needed. 63 Building on previous work demonstrating that brief, tablet-based training can support early 64 communicative abilities [10], the present study extends this approach by targeting a broader range 65 of linguistic structures, including verbs, adjectives, adverbs, and passive constructions. By more 66 closely approximating the complexity of natural language, this expanded intervention aims to 67 assess whether structured, supervised digital interactions can be effectively leveraged to support 68 early language development. 69 The primary objective of this study was to evaluate whether a short, educator-supervised tablet-70 based intervention is associated with improvements in language comprehension in toddlers and 71 preschoolers. Given the ongoing debate surrounding digital media use in early childhood, this work 72 seeks to contribute empirical evidence regarding the conditions under which such technologies may 73 support, rather than hinder, developmental outcomes. This perspective emphasizes that the 74 developmental impact of digital media depends less on exposure per se than on the quality and 75 context of its use. 76 77

Study Design 80 We implemented a quasi-experiment design, consisting of a training period with pre- and post-81 training evaluations of linguistic abilities. The study included toddlers (aged 2-3 years) and 82 preschoolers with the latter divided into two distinct cohorts: 3-to-4-year-olds and 4-to-5-year-olds. 83 These three groups (thereafter referred to by their respective age ranges) were analyzed separately 84 to account for developmental differences. 85 We assigned children to the study (hereafter Study) or Control group, balancing by pre-training 86 scores language scores, child’s sex and age and mother’s age and education. 87

We remark that 90 sanitary restrictions did not affect the comparison between study and control groups at the pre and 91 post training steps. Our study was approved by the institutional review board of the Pontificia 92 Universidad Católica de Chile. Parents provided written informed consent before their child 93 participated. 94 95 

Participants 96 Children were recruited from eight early childhood care centers in Santiago, Chile, between 2021 97 and 2023, a period characterized by pandemic-related health restrictions. All participants came from 98 low- to middle-socioeconomic-status, monolingual Spanish-speaking families. Children were born 99 full-term, exhibited typical developmental trajectories at the time of intervention, and had no 100 reported family history of language impairment or dyslexia. 101 Exclusion criteria included diagnosed visual or auditory impairments, neurodevelopmental 102 disorders, and chronic conditions associated with developmental delays. Children with neonatal 103 asphyxia, epilepsy, chromosomal abnormalities, or inborn errors of metabolism were excluded. 104 Of the 565 families invited, 332 provided consent to participate. The final analytical sample 105 consisted of 246 children who completed pre- and post-training evaluations for at least one 106 assessment battery. For those in the study groups, inclusion required participation in four to eight 107 training sessions. The final cohort comprised 87 toddlers (2-3 years), 85 preschoolers aged 3-4 108 years, and 74 aged 4-5 years. Participant dropout was largely due to public health restrictions 109 related to seasonal respiratory illnesses including SARS-CoV-2. The excluded and included children 110 did not differ in demographics characteristics (Supporting information Table S1). 111 

## 112 

Measures 113 Pre-post training evaluations included parental reports and direct child assessments using age-114 appropriate standardized batteries. 115 Parental reports included the Spanish versions of the Ages & Stages Questionnaires [11,12] 116 (hereafter ASQ-3), which evaluates Communication, Gross Motor, Fine Motor, Problem Solving, and 117 Personal-Social domains. ASQ-3 data was collected for all participants. 118 For 2-to-3-years, we also administered the short form of the MacArthur-Bates Communicative 119 Development Inventories [13,14] (hereafter CDI), a parent-report questionnaire assessing 120 expressive vocabulary (via a 100-word checklist) and early speech complexity (use of two-words 121 combinations). Additionally, a child psychologist administered the Receptive and Expressive 122 language scales of the Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development, 3 rd Edition, to each child. 123 For 3-to-4- and 4-to-5-years, a speech therapist administered the Screening Test of Spanish 124 Grammar [15] (hereafter, STSG) to evaluate receptive and expressive grammar. 125 Additionally, 3-to-4-years completed the Spanish version of the Test for Auditory Comprehension of 126 Language [16] (hereafter TACL), chosen for its age-appropriate, concise instructions regarding 127 vocabulary, morphology, and syntax. 4-to-5-years completed the Phonological Awareness 128 Assessment [17] (hereafter PAA) on an exploratory basis to estimate emergent pre-reading skills, 129 specifically syllabic and phonemic awareness. 130 Except for CDI, all assessment tools provide both raw scores and age-normed categorical 131 classifications. Since most participants fell within normative limits, we analyzed raw scores to 132 capture finer variations in performance. Child psychologists and speech therapists were external to 133 the research team and remained blind to the group assignation of each child. 134

Procedures 135 The procedure was similar to the one reported in (ref. 10). Pre- and post-training evaluations 136 involved parent-completed questionnaires and professional assessments administered by child 137 psychologists (toddlers) and speech therapists (preschoolers). To minimize bias, all evaluators were 138 external to the study and blinded to experimental conditions. 139 The training was administered individually and supervised by an early childhood educator in a quiet 140 room. During the training, a series of videos featuring an early childhood educator guided the 141 children in associating spoken words with their corresponding images (Fig. 1). Contrary to the 142 previous study (ref. 8), these associations spanned a diverse linguistic range, including nouns, 143 verbs, adjectives, and adverbs of place, manner, or quantity, as well as passive constructions. The 144 training consisted of four to eight 15-minute sessions, each including five trials. Each trial was 145 divided in two phases: encoding and recognition. During encoding, children established 146 associations between two spoken words and their corresponding images through interactive touch-147 screen prompts. During recognition, they were asked to match one of the trained words to one of 148 the two images presented simultaneously. Tactile responses were recorded and classified as 149 correct (target selection), omitted (no response), or incorrect (selection of the distractor or an area 150 outside the target boundaries). The training was guided by videos of an early childhood educator 151 who invited the child to play using direct child speech, direct gaze and smiling gestures during every 152 trial and provided immediate encouraging social feedback after each response, regardless of the 153 correctness. 154 In the preschool cohorts, trials concluded with a vocalization phase wherein the educator in the 155 video prompted the child to produce the target word. To encourage engagement to speak, any 156 speech-like vocalization was categorized as a correct response and replayed to the child as 157 auditory reinforcement. Conversely, omitted or incorrect responses triggered encouraging, non-158 corrective feedback. 159 

## 160 

Statistical Analysis 161 To assess the impact of the intervention, we computed standardized gain scores for each 162 assessment battery within each age group. Standardized gain was defined as the difference 163 between post- and pre-training raw scores divided by the pooled pre-training standard deviation of 164 the corresponding age group, irrespective of group assignment. The mean interval between 165 assessments was 59 days for toddlers and 60 days for preschoolers. 166 To account for non-normality in gain scores, we employed nonparametric rank-based methods. 167 Specifically, we used rank-based linear regression implemented in the Rfit package in R [18] to 168 evaluate the effect of the intervention while adjusting for potential confounding variables. Unlike 169 ordinary least squares regression, this approach is robust to violations of normality and to the 170 presence of outliers, as it minimizes Jaeckel’s dispersion function using Wilcoxon scores [19]. Given 171 the developmental nature of our sample, p-values and standard errors for regression coefficients ( β)172 were estimated using a permutation-based approach with 10,000 repetitions to ensure stable 173 inference. 174 The final analysis included all children who provided both pre- and post-intervention data for at least 175 one measure; participants in the intervention group were additionally required to complete a 176 minimum of four training sessions. We also conducted an exploratory analysis of vocalizations 177 produced by preschoolers in the intervention group. 178 Given the number of outcome measures and age-stratified analyses, the analyses were considered 179 exploratory. Accordingly, we did not apply formal corrections for multiple comparisons and instead 180 interpreted results with appropriate caution, emphasizing consistency across measures and effect 181 sizes rather than isolated p-values. 182 183

2-to-3-years 191 Analyses of the ASQ-3 (Fig. 2A) and CDI revealed no significant differences between groups, 192 suggesting that parental perceptions of general developmental progress were similar across both 193 groups. 194 In contrast, unadjusted analyses of the direct child assessment with Bayley Receptive Language 195 scale showed that the Study group achieved significantly higher gain scores than the Control group 196 (Median 0.52 vs. 0.15, Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney Z = -2.01, p = 0.044, r = -0.22; Fig. 2B). 197 To account for potential confounding bio-demographic factors, we conducted a rank-based 198 regression adjusting for toddler’s age and sex, as well as parental age and education. The effect of 199 Group remained robust after adjustment ( β = 0.49, SE = 0.18, p = 0.009), whereas no other 200 covariates reached significance (all p > 0.30) (Table 2, 2-to-3-years section), indicating that 201 language gains were significantly associated with the intervention. 202 So far, these results suggest that the Study group showed robust improvements in receptive 203 language abilities. While the intervention was associated with these gains, bio-demographic 204 variables, specifically child age and parental education, exerted negligible influence in our data. 205 206 

3-to-4-years 207 The Study group showed greater gains across all TACL subscales compared to the Control group 208 (Fig. 2C). Gains were significantly higher in Morphology (median = 0.81 vs. 0.12, Z = −2.29, p = 209 0.021, r = −0.25), Syntax (median = 1.23 vs. 0.41, Z = −1.97, p = 0.048, r = −0.21), and Vocabulary 210 (median = 0.77 vs. 0, Z = −3.41, p < 0.001, r = −0.37). The total linguistic gain, reported for clinical 211 comparison only, was also higher in the Study group (median = 0.82 vs. 0.13, Z = −3.19, p = 0.001, 212 r = −0.35). These results indicate that the intervention had a moderate effect on overall language 213 comprehension, with the largest effect observed in the Vocabulary subscale. 214 As in toddlers, rank-based regression confirmed that Group was the strongest predictor of gain, with 215 significant effects for Morphology ( Β = 0.679; p = 0.014) and Vocabulary ( β = 0.593; p = 0.018) 216 (Table 2, 3-to-4-year-olds section). Child age contributed to Vocabulary gain ( β = 0.242; p = 0.046), 217 but the effect of Group was more than twice as large, suggesting that the intervention was the 218 primary driver of improvements at this age. 219 No significant differences were found in gains on the ASQ-3 or STSG. The lack of differences in the 220 STSG is likely due to the longer and more complex instructions, which may be difficult for younger 221 preschoolers to comprehend. 222 Together, these results indicate that 3-to-4-year-olds benefited from the intervention in language 223 comprehension, with child age playing a secondary role. 224 225

Instead, 237 all Study groups showed small-to-moderate but consistent improvements in language 238 comprehension abilities. 239 Since no gains were observed in expressive language, we further examined vocalizations in the 240 Study group to obtain additional insight into the intervention’s impact on expressive language. 241 242 

Vocalization analysis 243 Significant changes in vocalizations were observed only in the 4-to-5-year-olds group. These 244 children spoke more, produced a greater number of content words, and used a wider variety of 245 grammatical words across sessions (Fig. 3A), suggesting that the intervention may provide a playful 246 context for practicing verbal behavior. 247 Additionally, across all preschoolers, the mean number of vocalizations was positively correlated 248 with mean accuracy in both the encoding and recognition phases (Figs 3B and 3C). This suggests 249 that children who more accurately encoded and recognized word-image associations also tended to 250 vocalize more frequently. This relationship may reflect underlying differences in linguistic ability or 251 task engagement, although further studies are needed to clarify the mechanisms involved. In the 252 absence of comparable vocalization data from a control group, these findings should be interpreted 253 as descriptive of in-task behavior rather than evidence of generalized improvements in expressive 254 language. 255 256 

ANCOVA analysis 257 Finally, we examined the possibility that the observed Group differences were due to pre-existing 258 disparities in linguistic skills by conducting nonparametric ANCOVAs. No significant differences 259 were found in any comparison (2-3 years, Bayley language comprehension, p = 0.298; 3-4 years, 260 TACL Morphology, p = 0.559; TACL Syntax, p = 0.202; TACL Vocabulary, p = 0.130; 4-5 years, 261 STSG language comprehension, p = 0.312). 262 263 

## Discussion 264 265 Our results indicate that a structured, tablet-based language intervention can be associated with 266 improvements in language comprehension across both toddlerhood and preschool years. Since the 267 groups were balanced at baseline and results remained consistent after adjustment, these 268 differences are unlikely to stem from preexisting disparities. While concerns regarding the 269 deleterious effects of digital media are prevalent [20], our findings indicate no such negative 270 outcomes. On the contrary, the observation of measurable gains following an intervention of only 271

## 8four to eight weeks suggests helpfulness. This relatively brief and structured format is particularly 272 well-suited for early childhood, as it prevents the cognitive overload and attentional fatigue often 273 associated with prolonged digital exposure, thereby maximizing the quality of engagement over its 274 quantity. 275 There is broad consensus that research in real-world settings using quasi-experiment designs can 276 yield small-to-moderate effect sizes that provide meaningful insights for clinical and educational 277 practice [21]. The interactive nature of our intervention, requiring active touchscreen input and 278 speech production rather than passive consumption, was likely a key driver of the gains we 279 observed. By featuring educator videos with eye contact and positive feedback, the pedagogical 280 design mimicked the social engagement crucial for early language acquisition. This approach likely 281 engaged multi-modal learning pathways, integrating auditory, visual, and motor inputs, fostering a 282 contingent interaction that serves as an active learning environment rather than a mere source of 283 digital exposure. 284 Overall, the intervention appears to have potentiated the natural developmental trajectory of 285 preschoolers. During this stage, vocabulary, morphology, and syntax evolve rapidly along distinct 286 paths. Vocabulary undergoes significant expansion not only in terms of lexical quantity [13] but also 287 in the depth of semantic integration and the complexity of word relations [22]. As vocabulary growth 288 is heavily contingent upon environmental quality [8,9], the tablet-based training served as a 289 valuable enrichment. In contrast, morphosyntactic development tends to progress more gradually 290 and exhibits less individual variability [23]. This relative stability likely stems from the fact that 291 grammar relies on complex temporal integration and rule-based mechanisms, such as abstraction 292 and generalization. Morphosyntax being partially mediated by vocabulary size, it is also highly 293 sensitive to the quality of environmental stimulation [9]. Furthermore, while vocabulary and syntax 294 are foundational to both comprehension and expression, speech production introduces the 295 additional cognitive demand of encoding thoughts into verbal messages within socially contingent 296 contexts. Further research with larger cohorts is required to determine whether intervention-driven 297 gains in one area might catalyze improvements in the others. 298 Globally, our results indicate that effects are strongest in direct assessments, while parent-report 299 measures may be less sensitive. The discrepancy observed between parent-reported measures 300 (ASQ-3) and professional assessments (Bayley) is well-documented [24,25]. Even in larger cohorts, 301 these correlations remain moderate at best, particularly when tracking nuanced longitudinal 302 changes rather than major developmental shifts. The discrepancy might have been exacerbated in 303 our study by the COVID-19 pandemic, where parents, sensitized by concerns over developmental 304 delays due to lockdowns, may have adopted a more pessimistic lens [26,27]. Clinically, this 305 divergence may also reflect a 'lag' in perception; receptive gains often remain 'invisible' to 306 caregivers until they surface as expressive speech. Furthermore, our data confirmed that parental 307 education was significantly associated with outcomes, particularly among older children [28-31]. 308 This highlights the role of environmental scaffolding, where the intervention's impact is influenced 309 by the preexisting linguistic capital of the household. 310 While consistent group differences in standardized expressive measures were not observed, 311 exploratory analyses of the Study group’s in-app vocalizations revealed that older preschoolers 312 increased both the frequency and diversity of their speech production. Furthermore, children with 313 higher task accuracy had greater vocalization rates. Although these behaviors did not correlate with 314 standardized gain scores, suggesting they may reflect task-specific engagement rather than 315 generalized linguistic advancement, the cultivation of verbal behavior remains clinically significant. 316 Proficient spoken language is a cornerstone of social and academic success; children with robust 317 expressive skills exhibit superior social competence, more positive peer interactions [32], and a 318 higher propensity for prosocial behavior [33]. 319 From a theoretical perspective, the present findings can be interpreted in light of empirically 320 supported mechanisms of social interaction in language learning. The importance of ostensive 321 social cues and communicative contexts in guiding early learning has been emphasized in 322 developmental research [34]. Research in language acquisition indicates that children learn words 323 more effectively when linguistic input occurs within episodes of shared attention, contingent 324

In this context, the 327 effectiveness of the present intervention may stem not from digital exposure per se, but from its 328 capacity to incorporate these interactional features in a playful, human-supervised environment. 329 Thus, the intervention can be understood as a socially enriched learning environment that supports 330 educator-child interactions rather than as a passive digital tool. 331 Despite these promising results, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, our assessment 332 took place immediately post-intervention; therefore, the long-term retention of these linguistic gains 333 remains to be determined through longitudinal follow-up. Second, while we emphasized the role of 334 the digital tool, the socio-technical interaction between the child, the tablet, and the supervising 335 adult likely contributed to the intervention's success. This aligns with evidence that digital learning is 336 most effective when the content is reinforced through real-world application during daily routines. 337 Future research should aim to disentangle the specific contributions of adult scaffolding versus 338 independent digital interaction to further refine and optimize educational protocols. 339 340 

## Conclusions 341 

## 342 Our study shows that a structured, tablet-based language intervention can yield small-to-moderate 343 improvements in language comprehension across both toddlerhood and the preschool years. These 344 gains remained robust after controlling child and parental factors, suggesting that the results are not 345 attributable to preexisting group differences. Critically, no negative effects were detected under 346 brief, supervised use, informing ongoing concerns about early digital media exposure. 347 We argue that the focus of public health and educational discourse should shift from technology 348 itself to the modality and context of its use. When human or financial resources are limited, these 349 devices offer a scalable and accessible alternative for developmental support. Furthermore, while 350 further research is required to map the transition from receptive to expressive gains, our exploratory 351 data suggest that older preschoolers may leverage the contingent nature of interactive tablets to 352 practice verbal behavior, a skill vital for social and academic success. Ultimately, tablet-based tools, 353 when pedagogically structured and supervised, represent a valuable complement to naturalistic 354 language development rather than a replacement for it. 355 356
