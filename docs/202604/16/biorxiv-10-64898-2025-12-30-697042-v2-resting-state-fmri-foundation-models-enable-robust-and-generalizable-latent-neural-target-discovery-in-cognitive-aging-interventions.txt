Abstract 1

The benefits of interventions targeting cognitive aging vary substantially across individuals, 2

largely owing to heterogeneity in aging-related comorbidities. It is necessary to robustly identify 3

neural patterns underlying intervention response and test their generalizability across 4

heterogeneous cohorts. Resting-state functional MRI (rsfMRI) offers a potential pathway, but 5

relying on predefined summary features with conventional methods has limited capacity to 6

capture both within-individual longitudinal variation and between-individual differences, 7

particularly in small and heterogeneous studies. Recent rsfMRI foundation models pretrained on 8

large observational cohorts present a promising alternative by learning transferable 9

spatiotemporal representations from time-series signals. Yet their validity and generalizability in 10 

local intervention settings remain unclear. Here, we systematically evaluated rsfMRI foundation 11 

models using data from two independent randomized controlled trials of older adults with mild 12 

cognitive impairment, testing whether these models can robustly extract longitudinal brain 13 

representations that predict post-intervention changes in episodic memory across trials. 14 

Foundation models outperformed conventional machine learning and deep learning approaches 15 

across both trials. Clinically informed adaptation using an external Alzheimer’s disease cohort 16 

further improved performance and robustness to confounders (i.e., head motion, site, and 17 

intervention arm), with accuracy up to 82%. Multivariate decomposition of foundation model 18 

embeddings identified latent neural patterns associated with episodic memory change with cross-19 

study consistency at baseline that became more spatially distributed at post-intervention. These 20 

findings show that rsfMRI foundation models can enable robust and generalizable identification of 21 

latent neural patterns linking longitudinal brain dynamics to individual intervention response, 22 

laying the foundation for precision-driven neural target discovery in cognitive aging research. 23 

Significance Statement 24 

Interventions for cognitive aging show highly variable outcomes across individuals, limiting their 25 

clinical effectiveness. This study introduces a foundation model-based approach to identify latent 26 

neural patterns underlying individual differences in intervention response using resting-state fMRI. 27 

By leveraging pretrained models and domain-adaptive fine-tuning, we demonstrate robust and 28 

generalizable prediction of cognitive improvement across independent trials. Our findings suggest 29 

that latent brain representations, rather than predefined features, provide a scalable pathway 30 

toward precision-driven intervention strategies for aging populations. 31

Introduction 34 

> 35

As the global population ages, scalable interventions with minimal side effects, such as cognitive 36 

training and aerobic exercise, has become a focus in clinical research aimed at preventing 37 

cognitive decline 1. However, the cognitive benefits of these interventions highly vary across 38 

individuals due to individual heterogeneity in aging-related comorbidities 2. This variability has 39 

been a primary contributor to a history of inconclusive or failed trials 3–5 . However, rather than 40 

viewing these failures as mere setbacks, they represent a critical source of data. The 41 

accumulation of mixed results underscores the need to move beyond a trial-and-error approach. 42 

By systematically analyzing data across diverse trials, it may be possible to identify robust, 43 

generalizable patterns of neuroplastic potential, i.e., the brain capacity for beneficial cognitive 44 

change, paving the way for the future more consistently effective and individually tailored 45 

strategies for cognitive enhancement. 46 

> 47

Resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rsfMRI) provides an objective approach to 48 

examine individuals’ neuroplastic potential 6,7 . Traditionally, the utility of rsfMRI for mechanistic 49 

understanding of interventions has relied on the assumption that neuroplastic potential from 50 

interventions manifest within pre-defined regions of interest (ROIs) or at the level of pairwise 51 

functional connectivity (FC) 8. This assumption imposes a restrictive representational framework. 52 

By aggregating BOLD signals into static ROI-level or connectivity summaries, such approaches 53 

may obscure complex, higher-order interactions among distributed neural components 9, where 54 

intervention-related mechanisms may instead be expressed in latent, multivariate patterns rather 55 

than localized changes in individual ROIs or discrete FC edges 10 . This limitation is particularly 56 

consequential in aging cohorts, where intervention-related neural responses tend to be 57 

heterogeneous, dynamic, and idiosyncratic, diminishing the sensitivity of fixed anatomical targets 58 

or predefined connectivity metrics for capturing individual-level response signatures 2. Analytically, 59 

many statistical and machine learning (ML) approaches to rsfMRI operate directly on these pre-60 

defined features 11 . Conversely, conventional deep learning (DL) that operate on raw time-series 61 

data often require massive datasets that are often unavailable in real-world clinical settings 12 . In 62 

limited-data regimes, DL models may either oversimplify temporal dependencies or overfit to 63 

noisy BOLD signals, further constraining their utility for mechanistic inference 13 .64 

> 65

To uncover these understudied latent, multivariate neural patterns robustly across studies, we 66 

propose a new approach that leverages Foundation Models (FMs) to learn brain representations 67 

directly from raw rsfMRI timeseries. FMs, which are pre-trained on vast and diverse datasets, 68 

learn fundamental, generalizable patterns of brain activity before being applied to specific 69 

downstream tasks 14–16 . The strength of this approach lies in three key areas: First, FM 70 

architectures, often Transformer-based, are explicitly designed to capture complex, long-range 71 

temporal dependencies in BOLD signals that are lost in static FC summaries 17 . Second, the 72 

extensive pre-training provides a strong inductive prior, enabling these models to extract 73 

meaningful spatiotemporal dynamics for smaller, local intervention datasets while mitigating 74 

overfitting. By pre-training on thousands of hours of rsfMRI data, FMs can distill robust and 75 

generalizable neural representations that preserve fine-grained spatiotemporal signatures of brain 76 

function 18,19 . Third, FM-derived embeddings offer a compact, low-dimensional space that can be 77 

linked to behavioral outcomes, facilitating the discovery of latent neural variables. This capacity is 78 

particularly useful in intervention studies of aging, where neural responses are heterogeneous 79 

and individualized. However, a systematic methodology for adapting FMs to challenging small-80 

sample settings, such as local, aging-focused intervention studies, has not yet been established. 81 

Therefore, the central goal of our work is to develop and validate such an approach, 82 

demonstrating how FMs can be reliably adapted to identify latent patterns of neuroplastic 83 

potential in older adults and support data-driven brain mechanism discovery. 84

In this paper, we aim to identify latent, multivariate neural patterns that are robust across 86 

intervention studies by adapting rsfMRI FMs and evaluating their utility in predicting individual 87 

cognitive responses in aging populations. Specifically, we address four research questions 88 

relevant to applying rsfMRI foundation models in local cognitive aging intervention studies: (Q1) 89 

whether FM-derived embeddings improve predictive performance over conventional approaches 90 

in small, heterogeneous cohorts; (Q2) whether clinically informed domain adaptation and joint 91 

modeling of baseline and post-intervention states enhance intervention-relevant representation; 92 

(Q3) whether FM-derived representations remain robust under common neuroimaging 93 

confounders including site variability, head motion, and intervention arm; and (Q4) whether their 94 

latent structure shows consistent organization across independent intervention cohorts. 95 

> 96

To answer these questions, we evaluated two representative rsfMRI FMs: BrainLM 18 , trained to 97 

model whole-brain activity as an inference sequence similar to a language model, and 98 

BrainJEPA 19 , designed to learn shared embeddings from neighboring brain states. Both rsfMRI 99 

FMs were originally pre-trained on large-scale datasets, such as UK Biobank 20 and the Human 100 

Connectome Project (HCP) 21 , which predominantly consist of younger, neurologically healthy 101 

adults. We further fine-tuned them using the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative 102 

(ADNI) 22 dataset to ensure the brain representations reflect the range of variability covering both 103 

normal and abnormal cognitive aging. We then tested the adapted FMs on two independent local 104 

intervention studies targeting cognitive aging: ACT 23 study (NCT03313895), a three-site 105 

randomized control trial (RCT) with four arms over six-month exercise and cognitive training 106 

combined, alone, or active control, and CogTE 24 study (NCT02559063), a single-site RCT with 107 

two arms over six-week cognitive training or active control. Notably, both trials exemplify a 108 

common challenge in aging research: neither study identified a statistically significant group-level 109 

improvement in their primary outcome, episodic memory (EM) in older adults with mild cognitive 110 

impairment (MCI). This very absence of a clear average treatment effect makes them ideal 111 

testbeds for our purpose. The mixed results highlight the failure of traditional group-level analyses 112 

and create a need for methods that can uncover the individual-level neural factors distinguishing 113 

those who benefited from those who did not. To this end, we used rsfMRI data collected at 114 

baseline and post-intervention from both studies. Change in EM ( ∆EM) was calculated with 115 

responders defined as sustained/improved post-intervention from baseline ( ΔEM>=0) vs. non-116 

responders as declined cases ( ΔEM<0). By focusing on individual-level outcome, we explicitly 117 

test FM-derived brain representations can capture latent, multivariate neural patterns that robustly 118 

explain variability in cognitive response across studies. The overview of the analytical flow is 119 

presented in Figure 1 .120

our case. This evaluation was performed on ACT and CogTE, respectively; sample 137

Domain adaptive fine-tuning on ADNI dataset 159 

> 160

To address Q2, we examined whether clinically informed adaptation and joint modeling of 161 

baseline and post-intervention rsfMRI improve intervention-relevant representations. Specifically, 162 

the best-performing BrainLM was fine-tuned using a supervised classification task distinguishing 163 

group with Alzheimer’s disease (AD) from healthy control (HC), named as BrainLM-ADNI. The 164 

design was motivated by the assumption that intervention-related EM improvement reflects, at 165 

least in part, a reversal or modulation of neural patterns of EM difference between AD and HC, 166 

making the HC-to-AD contrast a biologically meaningful axis for adaptation. Moreover, the 167 

differences between AD and HC are more neurobiologically distinct and stable than those 168 

between preclinical AD, such as MCI, offering a stronger supervisory signal for domain 169 

adaptation. 170

the same downstream inference protocol as in Section 2.1 . As shown in Figure 2b , BrainLM-173 

ADNI demonstrated a substantial performance boost, particularly on the more challenging ACT 174 

dataset ( CogTE: ACC 81.6% ± 5% [ ↑1.3% ], F1 88.8% ± 3% [ ↑0.6%] ); ACT: ACC 72.6% ± 13% 175

patterns learned from ADNI was successfully transferred, equipping the model with a more 177

Robustness to confounders concerning in neuroimaging and clinical trial studies 196 

> 197

To address Q3, we evaluated whether the adapted rsfMRI foundation model remains robust to 198 

common sources of heterogeneity in intervention studies, including site variability, head motion, 199 

and intervention arm. Using the multi-site ACT dataset, we evaluated the performance of 200 

BrainLM-ADNI under three preprocessing conditions: (1) original data, (2) data after site 201 

harmonization, and (3) data after both site harmonization and regression of head motion 202 

parameters. As shown in Figure 2c , explicit correction for these confounders resulted in only 203 

modest differences in classification performance ( original: ACC 72.6% ± 13%, F1 83.0% ± 10%; 204 

site-harmonized: ACC 73.2% ± 7%, F1 81.0% ± 8%; site + head-motion corrected: ACC 73.8% ± 205 

8%, F1 82.7% ± 6% ). Overall, these results indicate that explicit confound correction yields only 206 

modest performance changes, consistent with robustness to site-related heterogeneity and 207 

motion artifacts. 208 

> 209

Additionally, we examined whether classification performance differed across intervention arms 210 

(see Figure 2d ) in both studies. We performed a Chi-square test on intervention group 211 

assignment and classification performance (i.e., whether being correctly classified or not) for each 212 

participant. The classification performance displayed no significant difference across intervention 213 

arms for either study ( CogTE: χ²=0.01, p=0.92; ACT: χ²=5.44, p=0.14 ). These results suggest 214 

that BrainLM-ADNI maintains comparable classification performance across intervention arms, 215 

further supporting its robustness. 216

Discussion 252 

> 253

Summary of key findings across research questions 254 

> 255

The overarching goal of this work was to identify robust, latent multivariate neural patterns of 256 

neuroplastic potential that generalize across studies and explain individual differences in 257 

cognitive response. Our findings across four research questions converge to support this goal. 258 

First, rsfMRI FMs, particularly the ADNI-adapted BrainLM, consistently outperformed established 259 

ML approaches in predicting individual treatment response across two independent intervention 260 

studies, even in small and heterogeneous samples, indicating that FM-derived embeddings 261 

capture meaningful intervention-related signal beyond predefined features such as FC. This 262 

finding indicates that FM-derived embeddings capture meaningful intervention-related signal 263 

beyond noise and substantially beyond representations based on predefined features such as 264 

FC. Second, clinically informed domain adaptation via ADNI fine-tuning was essential, and jointly 265 

modeling baseline and post-intervention states provided complementary longitudinal information, 266 

suggesting that intervention effects are encoded at a latent spatiotemporal representational level 267 

rather than as localized or static changes. Third, these representations demonstrated robustness 268 

across studies with differing designs, populations, and intervention modalities, suggesting that the 269 

models capture generalizable neural patterns relevant to cognitive response rather than study-270 

specific artifacts. Importantly, architectural choice and domain adaptation played a critical role, 271 

with ADNI fine-tuning improving performance and BrainLM outperforming BrainJEPA, highlighting 272 

the importance of aligning pretrained representations with aging-related neural variability. Finally, 273 

interpretable embedding-brain relationships revealed consistent differentiation between 274 

responders and non-responders with structured cross-study organization, providing evidence that 275 

rsfMRI FMs can reliably identify latent neural patterns relevant to cognitive intervention outcomes. 276 

> 277

Identifying robust latent neural patterns with rsfMRI FMs in local intervention studies 278 

> 279

We emphasize a three-condition framework for establishing robust latent, multivariate 280 

neural patterns in cognitive aging intervention research: First, the analytical model must learn 281 

brain representations that preserve fine-grained spatiotemporal structure beyond predefined 282 

features, despite noise, heterogeneity and limited sample size; Second, these representations 283 

must demonstrate behavioral relevance by meaningfully relating to individual cognitive change 284 

following intervention; Third, the identified neural representations must exhibit consistency across 285 

independent studies, indicating shared neural signature rather than study-specific artifacts. 286 

Our findings satisfy the first condition. FM-derived embeddings captured subtle spatiotemporal 287 

patterns that are difficult to recover from predefined connectivity features or models trained from 288 

scratch on small intervention datasets, a critical advantage in aging populations where 289 

intervention effects are modest and embedded within large inter-individual variability. Importantly, 290 

large-scale pretraining alone was insufficient; ADNI-based fine-tuning acted as an essential 291 

intermediate step, aligning general representations with aging-related neural variance and 292 

enabling transfer to intervention settings. 293 

> 294

The second condition was met through PLS-based latent component generation, which mapped 295 

FM-derived embeddings onto low-dimensional latent variables directly coupled to behavioral 296 

outcomes. Unlike fixed anatomical targets or predefined connectivity metrics, these latent 297 

variables allow intervention-related variation to emerge as distributed network-level patterns, 298 

which are well-suited to aging populations where neural changes are diffuse, modest, and not 299 

easily localized to canonical regions. Each selected PLS component demonstrated significant 300

The third condition was supported by structured cross-study consistency in PLS loading patterns. 304 

At baseline, correspondence was concentrated in a focal set of highest-weighted regions 305 

anchored in posterior medial cortex, reflecting shared neurobiological constraints across aging 306

Looking forward, an important next step is to aggregate such latent neural signatures across 313 

diverse intervention modalities (e.g., aerobic exercise, neuromodulation, mindfulness, and 314 

cognitive training) to construct a shared reference space of intervention-associated brain 315 

patterns. Within this space, FM-derived embeddings could serve as a common coordinate system 316 

for comparing individuals and interventions, enabling evaluation of how an individual’s baseline 317 

neural representation aligns with patterns associated with different response profiles. While this 318 

application is prospective, the framework outlines a path from post-hoc analyses to prescriptive 319 

medicine: a scalable, data-driven approach to personalizing interventions based on an 320 

individual’s latent neuroplastic potential, not on group-average effects. 321 

> 322

Practical considerations for identifying robust latent neural patterns with rsfMRI FMs 323 

> 324

For cognitive aging intervention research to make cumulative progress toward mechanistic insight 325 

and personalized treatment, it is essential for the field to consistently identify and compare latent, 326 

multivariate neural patterns across studies, rather than relying on isolated within-trial findings. In 327 

this context, applying rsfMRI FMs in intervention studies is best understood as a practical strategy 328 

for satisfying the three conditions required for robust latent pattern identification, rather than as a 329 

single fixed pipeline. Under realistic constraints such as limited sample size, heterogeneous 330 

responses, and domain mismatch, each condition can be supported by both the approach used 331 

here and alternative solutions. For the first condition, we used pre-trained rsfMRI FMs operating 332 

on BOLD time series and further adapted them using ADNI to better align the representation 333 

space with aging-related neural variation. Alternative solutions could include other pretrained 334 

sequence, graph-based 32 , or multimodal models 33 , to preserve fine-grained neural dynamics 335 

beyond predefined summary features. For the second condition, we evaluated whether FM-336 

derived embeddings predicted intervention response and used multivariate decomposition to 337 

identify latent components coupled to EM change. Alternative approaches could include other 338 

brain-behavior linkage methods, such as canonical correlation analysis or supervised latent 339 

variable models, to retain representations that explain meaningful individual cognitive variation, 340 

not just model performance. For the third condition, we assessed consistency across two 341 

independent intervention cohorts and examined robustness to confounders. Alternative 342 

approaches may include cross-dataset transfer, embedding alignment, or external validation, 343 

where identified patterns reflect shared neural structure rather than study-specific artifacts. In 344 

practice, these steps involve trade-offs among data access, model scale, interpretability, and 345 

engineering effort. 346

> 352

To systematically evaluate whether rsfMRI foundation models can support robust and 353 

generalizable identification of intervention-relevant latent neural patterns in cognitive aging 354

rsfMRI FM approaches. 370

Study cohorts and preprocessing 372 

Three independent datasets were included in this work for model deployment and evaluation. The 373 

ADNI dataset was used exclusively for domain-adaptive fine-tuning of the FM. The ADNI dataset 374 

comprises older adults spanning the cognitive spectrum from cognitively normal, MCI, to AD. In 375 

this study, ADNI participants diagnosed with AD and cognitively healthy controls were selected to 376 

construct a supervised fine-tuning task that captures aging- and pathology-related brain patterns. 377 

No ADNI data were included in the downstream model evaluation. The ACT study is a multi-site 378 

randomized controlled trial involving older adults at risk for AD, with participants assigned to 379 

aerobic exercise, cognitive training, combined intervention, or active control arms over six 380 

months. Resting-state fMRI and episodic memory assessments were collected at T1 and T2. The 381 

CogTE study is a single-site randomized controlled trial with a six-week cognitive training 382 

intervention and an active control condition, following a similar pre-post assessment design. Both 383 

ACT and CogTE datasets target older adults with elevated risk for cognitive decline and serve as 384 

independent cohorts for evaluating model generalizability across intervention paradigms and 385 

study designs (see Figure 3a ). Both datasets exhibited substantial inter-individual variability in 386 

EM change, with samples of responders and non-responders in each intervention arm (see 387 

Supplementary Materials Table S1 ). 388

MRI acquisition details for ADNI, ACT, and CogTE have been reported previously and are 390 

summarized in Supplementary Materials Table S1 . All imaging data were preprocessed using 391 

the standardized fMRIprep pipeline 34 (version 24.1.0), which is based on Nipype v1.8.6. For each 392 

participant, T1-weighted anatomical images underwent skull stripping, tissue segmentation, 393 

surface reconstruction, and spatial normalization to Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) space. 394 

RsfMRI images were corrected for head motion and slice timing, co-registered to the anatomical 395 

image, and normalized to MNI space. Susceptibility distortion correction was applied for the ACT 396 

dataset using estimated field maps from two echo-planar imaging references with TOPUP 35 that 397 

were available only for this study. Following fMRIPrep , additional denoising was performed using 398 

Nilearn (version 0.11.0). This included regression of twelve head motion parameters, five 399 

principal components derived from white matter and cerebrospinal fluid signals using the 400 

aCompCor approach, and temporal band-pass filtering (0.01–0.1 Hz). Finally, spatial smoothing 401 

was applied using a 6-mm full-width-at-half-maximum Gaussian kernel. Identical preprocessing 402 

procedures were applied across all datasets to minimize confounding differences arising from 403 

data handling and to ensure consistency in downstream model evaluation. 404

BrainLM is designed to model whole-brain resting-state activity as an inference sequence, 408 

analogous to autoregressive language modeling. During pretraining, BrainLM learns to predict 409 

future spatiotemporal patterns of brain activity conditioned on past observations, enabling it to 410 

capture long-range temporal dependencies and distributed neural dynamics. BrainLM operates 411 

directly on preprocessed rsfMRI time-series data, where voxel- or parcel-wise BOLD signals are 412 

temporally segmented into fixed-length patches of 20 datapoints and embedded as input tokens 413 

to the transformer encoder. Although three pretrained variants (13-/111-/650-million weights) 414 

were described in the original paper, only the 13-million weight model was publicly released and 415 

was therefore used in this study. 416 

> 417

BrainJEPA follows a joint embedding inference architecture. Its primary objective is to learn 418 

shared latent representations by enforcing consistency between embeddings derived from 419 

neighboring spatiotemporal views of the brain. BrainJEPA is trained to align representations of 420 

partially observed brain states, encouraging invariance across local temporal or spatial 421 

perturbations. As a result, it emphasizes representation consistency and local coherence rather 422 

than explicit temporal forecasting. BrainJEPA takes preprocessed rsfMRI time-series as input, 423 

formatting the signals into sequences of 160 time points and segmenting them into non-424 

overlapping patches of size 16. Although three pretrained variants (22-/86-/307-million weights) 425 

were described in the original paper, only the 22-million weight model was publicly released and 426 

was therefore used in this study. 427 

> 428

To deploy these rsfMRI FMs to local intervention studies without retraining the backbone, a linear 429 

probing strategy was employed. For each participant, rsfMRI data from T1 and T2 were 430 

independently passed through the frozen FM using shared weights. From each session, the final 431 

hidden representation corresponding to the Classification (CLS) token was extracted as a fixed-432 

dimensional embedding. These embeddings summarize the participant’s brain state at each 433 

timepoint in the learned representation space. The T1 and T2 embeddings were then 434 

concatenated to form a longitudinal feature vector explicitly encoding pre-post neural change. 435 

This fused representation was used as input to a lightweight, task-specific classifier trained to 436 

> ∆

EM. During linear probing, all FM weights remained frozen, and only the weights of the 437 

downstream classifier were optimized. This setup isolates the representational capacity of the 438 

pretrained rsfMRI FMs and allows a controlled comparison of their ability to extract longitudinal 439 

representations that support both intervention-response prediction and downstream discovery of 440 

latent brain-behavior patterns in small-sample intervention datasets ( Figure 3b ). Detailed training 441 

configurations are described in Supplementary Materials .442

Model fine-tuning 444 

> 445

To adapt the pretrained rsfMRI FM to aging-related neural variation before downstream 446 

intervention analysis, supervised fine-tuning was performed on the ADNI dataset as a clinically 447 

informed representation adaptation step. This fine-tuning step was applied exclusively to 448 

BrainLM, based on its superior transfer performance in zero-shot and linear probing settings. 449 

Fine-tuning was formulated as a binary classification task distinguishing participants diagnosed 450 

with AD vs. HC. This task was selected to introduce aging- and pathology-relevant supervisory 451 

signals into the pretrained representation space, without altering the downstream intervention 452 

datasets. Only ADNI data were used during fine-tuning, and no ADNI subjects were included in 453 

any downstream evaluation. To reduce overfitting risk and computational cost, lightweight Low-454 

Rank Adaptation (LoRA) modules were inserted into the query, key, value, and output projection 455 

layers of the self-attention blocks in the BrainLM transformer. Most pretrained FM weights 456 

remained frozen, while the final transformer block was unfrozen to allow limited adaptation of 457 

higher-level representations. It enables the model to incorporate domain-specific information 458 

while preserving the general spatiotemporal representations learned during large-scale 459 

pretraining. During fine-tuning, the downstream classifier consisted of a two-layer MLP operating 460 

on the CLS token embedding produced by the BrainLM. Model optimization was conducted using 461

Compared ML and DL methods 470 

> 471

Support Vector Machine (SVM) served as a strong traditional machine learning method. For each 472 

participant, FC matrices were computed separately for T1 and T2 rsfMRI scans. The upper 473 

triangular elements of each FC matrix were flattened into feature vectors and concatenated 474 

across timepoints to explicitly encode longitudinal change. The resulting feature vector was used 475 

as input to a linear SVM classifier (see Figure 3d ). A linear kernel was chosen to reduce 476 

overfitting risk under limited sample sizes. The regularization parameter  was optimized via grid 477 

search within the training folds of the cross-validation procedure. 478 

> 479

Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) represents a vector-based deep learning method capable of 480 

modeling nonlinear relationships. The input features were the same as those used for the SVM, 481 

consisting of concatenated upper-triangular FC vectors from T1 and T2. The MLP architecture 482 

comprised three fully connected layers with ReLU activation functions and dropout for 483 

regularization (see Figure 3d ). Hyperparameters, including hidden layer dimensions, dropout 484 

rates, and learning rate, were tuned within the training folds to mitigate overfitting. 485 

> 486

Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) was implemented to explicitly incorporate brain network 487 

topology and model longitudinal graph-structured data. Brain regions of interest (ROIs) were 488 

treated as nodes in a graph, with edges defined by a shared adjacency matrix constructed from a 489 

control-group functional connectivity template. For each participant, node features were derived 490 

from FC matrices at T1 and T2, resulting in two graph inputs representing baseline and post-491 

intervention brain states. A dual-stream GCN architecture with shared weights was used to 492 

process the T1 and T2 graphs in parallel. Each stream consisted of stacked graph convolutional 493 

layers followed by graph-level pooling to produce timepoint-specific embeddings. These 494 

embeddings were then fused by concatenation along with their difference to explicitly encode 495 

longitudinal change. The fused representation was passed to a fully connected output layer for 496 

classification (see Figure 3d ). Model parameters were optimized using the Adam optimizer, and 497 

training was conducted within the same stratified cross-validation framework as all other models. 498
