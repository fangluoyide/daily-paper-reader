## Abstract 40 Cognitive aging is widely associated with a progressive weakening of predictive brain 41 mechanisms. This view is supported by decades of electrophysiological studies reporting 42 attenuated mismatch responses in older adults. Yet the literature remains inconsistent, 43 suggesting that aging may not uniformly attenuate predictive processing. One possibility is 44 that multiple predictive subsystems operate concurrently but have rarely been disentangled. 45 Here we address this question by separating whole-brain networks underlying predictive 46 processing in source-reconstructed magnetoencephalography (MEG) data from 77 younger 47 and older adults performing the auditory local–global paradigm. Network decomposition 48 revealed three temporally overlapping predictive subsystems with distinct functional profiles. 49 Aging exerted selective effects across these networks. Sensory prediction error responses 50 were enhanced within a network linking auditory cortices with medial cingulate regions, 51 whereas responses associated with reorientation of attention and contextual pattern 52 processing were attenuated in older adults. The level of multivariate recurrency across these 53 networks was preserved with aging, while the processing of sensory violations induced more 54 recurrency and less divergence relative to contextual violations in both groups. These 55 findings challenge the prevailing view that predictive processing simply declines with age. 56 Instead, aging redistributes predictive resources across distinct neural systems, amplifying 57 sensory-based processes while weakening more cognitively demanding predictive 58 mechanisms. 59 60

## Introduction 64 Predictive coding posits that perception arises from the brain’s continuous attempts to 65 anticipate incoming sensory signals and that internal models are updated when sensory 66 evidence violates those expectations. Such mismatches generate prediction errors at multiple 67 hierarchical and temporal scales within the nervous system 1-6 . In the auditory domain, 68 predictive processing spans a broad range, from local predictions about immediate tone 69 features to global expectations about abstract, higher-order regularities unfolding over 70 extended time windows 7-9 .71 Age-related changes in predictive processing have been studied most extensively at the 72 local sensory level. The prevailing view is that aging weakens prediction error signalling, 73 particularly for simple sensory violations. This interpretation is supported by numerous 74 electrophysiological studies reporting reduced mismatch responses in older adults and has 75 often been framed as an age-related shift in the balance between top-down priors derived 76 from internal models and bottom-up evidence sampled through sensory input channels 10-18 .77 As sensory systems deteriorate with age, incoming signals may become less precise, 78 encouraging the brain to rely more heavily on prior expectations and less on noisy incoming 79 sensory input 19,20 . In such a framework, attenuated prediction error signalling would be an 80 adaptive consequence of reduced sensory reliability 21 .81 Yet this account remains incomplete. Findings across the aging literature are not fully 82 consistent with this account, and the evidence for a uniform decline in prediction error 83 signalling is weaker than often assumed. Although many studies report reduced mismatch 84 negativity (MMN) responses in older adults 17,22-25 , meta-analytic work also finds that a 85 substantial number of experiments detect no age-related MMN reduction 16 . In several cases, 86 preserved mismatch responses have been attributed to altered recruitment of attention-related 87 neural processes 15,26-32 . Similarly, in the relatively few studies using paradigms that probe 88 both local sensory violations and higher-order pattern violations, age effects have not been 89 uniform across levels. While MMN may be preserved, later responses linked to conscious 90 contextual updating and attentional control, such as the global prediction error (GPE) and 91 reorientation of attention (RON) responses, appear more consistently attenuated with 92 age 7,8,21,33-35 . This pattern raises the possibility that aging does not uniformly reduce 93 predictive processing but rather alters how distinct predictive subsystems are engaged. 94 A major limitation of prior work is that most studies have relied on scalp-level magneto-95 or electroencephalography (M/EEG) analyses 36,37 and on a component-centric logic focused 96

These responses are useful markers, but they do not directly reveal the large-98 scale brain networks that generate them, nor how such networks may differ across age. 99 Source-localisation studies suggest that local violations recruit bilateral fronto-temporo-100 parietal regions, including Heschl’s gyrus and surrounding auditory cortex 7,44 , whereas global 101 violations engage broader fronto-parieto-temporal systems overlapping with global 102 workspace accounts of conscious access 7,45-47 . However, these network-level systems have 103 only rarely been disentangled in relation to specific predictive responses and have not been 104 compared across age in order to characterise their temporal dynamics. 105 To address this gap, we apply a recently developed method, BROADband Network 106 Estimation via Source Separation (BROAD-NESS) 48,49 , to separate simultaneous, orthogonal 107 brain networks from source-reconstructed MEG data. This approach makes it possible to 108 move beyond single sensor-level components and identify concurrent large-scale networks 109 underlying predictive processing. Importantly, BROAD-NESS also enables a dynamical 110 characterisation of these networks. By embedding the dominant component time series into a 111 low-dimensional phase space, brain activity can be described as trajectories through a shared 112 network state space. Recurrence quantification analysis applied to these trajectories provides 113 quantitative indices of stability, predictability, persistence, and complexity, revealing how 114 often the system revisits similar network states over time. This allows us to ask not only 115 which networks support prediction error signalling, but also whether the dynamical regime of 116 predictive processing differs between sensory and pattern violations and whether these 117 distinctions are preserved in aging. 118 In the present study, we applied BROAD-NESS to source-reconstructed MEG data from 119 younger and older adults performing the auditory local–global paradigm. Our aims were 120 threefold: (i) to identify the dominant whole-brain networks supporting hierarchical 121 prediction error signalling; (ii) to reconstruct group-level network time series in order to 122 relate these networks to canonical event-related field (ERF) responses; and (iii) to quantify 123 how the phase-space dynamics and recurrence structure of these networks differ between 124 sensory and pattern violations and across age. By doing so, we test the conventional decline 125 account, which posits that predictive processes attenuate with age independent of task 126 demands and characterize the network configurations underlying these processes. 127 128 129 130

Overview of the experimental design and MEG source reconstruction 132 Seventy-seven participants engaged in an adapted version of the auditory local-global 133 paradigm 7 during magnetoencephalography (MEG) recordings (Fig. 1a-b). The task consisted 134 of 160 trials per block for a total of 4 blocks. Each block included a combination of patterns 135 consisting of either five repetitive tones, or patterns where the fifth tone deviated in 136 frequency (Fig. S1). The local-global paradigm was designed to elicit local neural responses 137 from sensory violations at the single tone level and global neural responses from violations at 138 pattern level across blocks. The paradigm shifted between two block types, where the number 139 of occurrences where the fifth tone differed in frequency was changed to create a global 140 violation of the expected pattern. In both block types, a target tone requiring a button press 141 was also presented to ensure continued attention towards the paradigm. 142 After performing pre-processing of the MEG data (see Methods for details), we computed 143 source reconstruction and obtained the difference wave by subtracting the processing of 144 standard stimuli from that of deviant stimuli before. We employed a single-shell forward 145 model alongside a beamforming approach as the inverse solution, using an 8-mm grid that 146 corresponded to 3,559 brain voxels (Fig. 1c-d). This procedure produced a time series for 147 each reconstructed brain voxel returning a matrix of 3,559 brain voxels x time-points, 148 independently for each participant and experimental condition. 149

Effective dimensionality (ED) 50 was computed from the PCA 175 eigenvalue spectrum to quantify the number of components that meaningfully contribute to 176 the data (see Methods for details). This procedure identified three significant PCs (ED = 177 2.835), which accounted for 51.78 %, 24.57 % and 15.12 % of the variance in the data. Phase space and recurrence quantification analysis 234 To further characterise the temporal organisation of BROAD-NESS-derived brain network 235 dynamics, we embedded the three dominant networks into a joint phase space and quantified 236 their recurrence structure. Visual inspection of the three-dimensional trajectories (Fig. 3a) 237 revealed more sharply defined, time-locked utilisation of brain networks in the local 238 condition relative to the global condition. In the local deviation condition in the time range of 239 150 to 400 ms post onset of the fifth tone, younger adults exhibited a stronger co-activation 240 between all three brain networks, whereas older adults relied more heavily on Brain Network 241 #1. When specifically examining age group differences in the three-dimensional phase space 242 trajectories of the local condition, it became apparent, that during the timing of the early local 243 MMN and P3a responses, older adults utilised Brain Network #1 to a larger extent, while this 244 network was more involved for younger adults during the late local RON response (Fig. 3b). 245 Furthermore, in both the three and two-dimensional phase space trajectories a more distinct 246 coactivation and deactivation of brain networks was observed in the local condition compared 247 to the global condition. Similarly, when inspecting the phase space trajectories of two of the 248 three brain networks against each other in all three possible combinations, younger adults 249 exhibited a more sharply defined time specific coactivation and deactivation of singular brain 250 networks compared to slower and less distinct transitions observed among older adults (Fig. 251 S3). 252 When inspecting the thresholded recurrence plots derived from these trajectories (Fig. 3c), 253 there was clear tendency for larger recurrency in the local condition across groups. 254 Recurrency here refers to how often a previously occupied state is revisited reflecting how 255 often the system’s dynamics return to similar configurations over time. For eight central 256 recurrence metrics (such as recurrence rate, trapped time and entropy), effects of age group, 257 condition and interaction was examined. All metrics showed significant condition effects (all 258 FDR corrected p-values ≤ 1.6 × 10  ), with no significant main effects of group and no 259 significant group × condition interactions (all FDR corrected p-values ≥ .16). Specifically, all 260 metrics except divergence were increased in the local compared to the global condition, 261 whereas divergence was increased in the global condition. These results indicate that, relative 262 to global pattern processing, the local deviance condition is associated with more recurrent, 263 predictable and temporally persistent network trajectories, while global processing is 264 characterised by faster separation of trajectories, less stable, and more exploratory dynamics. 265

Brain network modularity analysis 335 To assess whether the large-scale brain networks engaged by the local–global paradigm were 336 more constrained or more broadly distributed, we applied BROAD-NESS at the individual 337 level and derived effective dimensionality from the eigenspectrum of the resulting covariance 338 matrices. ED quantifies the effective number of orthogonal components contributing to the 339 signal, with higher values indicating broader and less constrained network involvement. 340 Across all groups and conditions, the cumulative variance explained increased steeply for 341 the first principal components before gradually approaching an asymptote (Fig. 5a), 342 indicating that a limited subset of components accounted for the dominant whole-brain 343 signal. However, the rise in explained variance was consistently delayed in the global 344 compared to the local condition. This pattern suggests that processing global stimulus 345 regularities involved a larger set of orthogonal network components. 346 At the individual level, ED varied substantially across participants (Fig. 5b). Nevertheless, 347 ED was higher in the global condition (M = 5.67, SD = 1.40) than in the local condition (M = 348 3.94, SD = 1.12), with 71 out of 77 participants showing this pattern. The within-subject 349 difference (global − local) showed a mean of 1.74 (SD = 1.37). 350 A 2 × 2 mixed ANOVA with Condition (Local, Global) as a within-subject factor and 351 Group (Younger, Older) as a between-subject factor revealed a robust main effect of 352 Condition, 353 F(1,75) = 121.34, p < .001, partial η² = .62, indicating substantially higher ED in the global 354 condition. The effect size was large, suggesting that approximately 62 % of the explainable 355 within-subject variance was attributable to condition differences. There was no evidence for a 356 main effect of Group, F(1,75) = 0.08, p = .779, partial η² = .001, nor for a Group × Condition 357 interaction, F(1,75) = 0.08, p = .780, partial η² = .001. Thus, the increase in ED from local to 358 global conditions was comparable between younger and older adults (Fig. 5c). Condition 359 effects remained significant after Bonferroni correction (p < 0.001), while Group and Group 360 x Condition effects remained insignificant (p = 1 in both cases). Although Anderson–Darling 361 tests indicated deviations from normality in the local condition within each age group, the 362 mixed ANOVA is generally robust to moderate non-normality in balanced designs. 363 Moreover, the effect of Condition was highly consistent across participants, supporting the 364 stability of the observed effect. 365

## Discussion 453 

Summary 454 In the present study, we applied BROAD-NESS, a PCA-based broadband network 455 decomposition framework, to source-reconstructed MEG data acquired from the auditory 456 local-global paradigm, where both sensory and pattern violations occur. Our two primary 457 aims were to characterise hierarchical prediction error signalling within large-scale brain 458 networks and its multivariate embedding in phase space and examine how these processes are 459 altered in healthy aging. Three dominant orthogonal brain networks obtained from the first 460 three principal components accounted for 91.5 % of variance in the data and were robustly 461 recoverable across alternative PCA implementation strategies. Three key findings emerged in 462 the study. First, automatically elicited prediction error signalling towards sensory deviancy 463 was increased in aging and relied on a primary network connecting bilateral auditory regions 464 to Medial and Posterior Cingulate Cortices strongly associated with the recruitment of 465 attentional neural resources. Conversely, neural responses indexing the reorientation of 466 attention following sensory violations and prediction error responses from global pattern 467 violations were attenuated with age. Second, the processing of local violations involved a 468 more recurrent and deterministic recruitment of the three main brain networks across time, 469 while the processing of global violations was more divergent and exploratory. Third, the 470 processing of global pattern violations was characterised by higher effective dimensionality 471 of principal components. This suggested involvement of a wider range of orthogonal brain 472 networks across the brain, compared to local violation processing which was more confined 473 to fewer brain networks. 474 Together, these findings nuance existing predictive coding models of aging, where 475 predictive processes indexed by ERF responses are expected to uniformly attenuate in 476

Large-scale network organisation of hierarchical prediction errors 484 We applied BROAD-NESS to source-reconstructed MEG data, and revealed three significant 485 PCs that accounted for 51.78 %, 24.57 %, and 15.12 % of the variance in the data and 486 displayed distinct network characteristics. Aging-related hierarchical alterations in prediction error signalling 522 Our primary aging-related finding concerned the pronounced alterations in ERF responses 523 observed with age and the underlying brain networks supporting these processes. Dynamical regimes depend on the hierarchical level of prediction error signalling 595 Beyond analyses of ERF responses, BROAD-NESS enabled a dynamical systems 596 characterisation of brain network trajectories. Phase-space embeddings of network time series 597 revealed that local deviants elicited comprised, recurrent trajectories, whereas global deviants 598 induced more dispersed trajectories. Recurrence quantification analysis confirmed robust 599 condition effects across all included metrics, with local processing showing higher 600 recurrence, determinism, trapping time, diagonal length, max vertical, laminarity, and 601 entropy, while global processing showed higher divergence. These findings are theoretically 602 consistent with hierarchical predictive coding accounts. Local deviants violate short-lived 603 sensory regularities and can be processed through relatively confined, recurrent circuits that 604 give rise to easily identifiable and pronounced ERF responses. Global deviants require 605 integration over longer time scales, revision of higher-order predictions, and engagement of 606 conscious access mechanisms associated with the activation of global workspace systems 607 across wide-ranging brain networks within the cortex 7,45,72-76 . A larger network organization observed in global deviance processing 614 Similarly, ED analysis provided converging evidence that the processing of global pattern 615 violations engages a broader network recruitment across the brain. Source reconstruction 778 MEG is a powerful tool in detecting whole-brain activity with excellent temporal resolution. 779 However, to fully understand the brain activity involved in complex cognitive tasks, it is 780 essential to identify the spatial sources of this activity. This requires solving the inverse 781 problem, as the MEG recordings reflect neural signals from outside the head but do not 782 directly indicate which brain regions generated them. To address this, we employed 783 beamforming algorithms 87-89 , using a combination of in-house-developed codes alongside the 784 OSL, SPM, and FieldTrip toolboxes. This head model 787 treats each brain source as an active dipole and describes how the activity of such a dipole 788 would be detected across MEG sensors 90,91 . The MEG data was co-registered with individual 789 T1-weighted MRI scans, using the 3D digitizer information to align the data. When 790 individual anatomical scans were unavailable, an MNI152-T1 template with 8-mm spatial 791 resolution was used to compute the forward model. 792

Statistical analysis 888 After computing PCA on the group averaged data, we extracted the time series of the 889 resulting brain networks for each participant in each condition (Local, Global) 48 . At each 895 time point, we tested (i) a between-subject Group effect by comparing younger and older 896 adults after averaging across conditions, (ii) a within-subject Condition effect by contrasting 897 the global versus local condition across all participants, and (iii) a Group × Condition 898 interaction defined as the difference in Global–Local contrasts between groups. Statistical 899 inference was performed using cluster-based permutation tests over time with 5,000 900 permutations, where cluster-mass statistics controlled the family-wise error rate. Clusters of 901 neighbouring supra-threshold time points were first defined using a pointwise cluster-forming 902 threshold of p < 0.05. For each PC and each effect, we obtained binary 907 significance masks and corresponding time windows of significant clusters, which were then 908 used to visualize coloured intervals indicating periods of significant group, condition, or 909 interaction effects. Spatial gradient embedding and clustering analysis of network topographies 996 To examine the spatial organisation of the dominant brain networks identified via the 997 BROAD-NESS framework, we performed a spatial gradient embedding and clustering 998 analysis on a voxel-wise activation map. This analysis was conducted on the data averaged 999 across participants to obtain a robust, group-level representation of network topographies. We 1000 focused on the first three principal components (PC #1 – PC #3), as they represented the most 1001 important brain networks given calculations of effective dimensionality. For each component, 1002 we thresholded the voxel-wise spatial weights by setting values between mean ± 1 standard 1003 deviation to zero. This allowed us to retain only the most strongly contributing voxels. These 1004 thresholded values were then used to define a three-dimensional (3D) embedding space, 1005 where the position of each voxel was determined by its weight on PC #1 (x-axis), PC #2 (y-1006 axis) and PC #3 (z-axis). This provided a gradient-like spatial representation, capturing how 1007 individual voxels contributed to the three brain networks. To identify spatially coherent voxel 1008 clusters within this 3D space, we applied k-means clustering 104 to the embedded voxel 1009 coordinates. Prior to clustering, the data were z-scored to normalise both axes and ensure 1010 equal weighting of the three components. For each value of k (ranging from 2 to 40) 105 , we 1011 computed the k-means clustering in 100 repetitions with different random initialisations to 1012 minimise the effect of convergence to local minima 104 . To determine the optimal number of 1013 clusters, we computed the silhouette coefficient 106 for each clustering solution. This metric 1014 assesses how well-separated the resulting clusters are by comparing the average distance 1015 between points within the same cluster to the average distance between points in 1016 neighbouring clusters.
