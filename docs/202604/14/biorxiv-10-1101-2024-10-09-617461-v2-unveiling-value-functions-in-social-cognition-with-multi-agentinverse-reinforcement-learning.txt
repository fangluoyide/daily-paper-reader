# Abstract 

Social behavior requires individuals to consider not only their own goals but also those of others. Latent value functions that encode such goals can be recovered from behavior using inverse reinforcement learning in single-agent settings. However, extending it to multi-agent interactions is challenging, because value functions are defined over joint state spaces that grow exponentially with the number of agents. Existing approaches often manage this complexity by imposing strong structural assumptions about social interactions, thereby limiting their applicability and inter-pretability. Here we show that joint value functions governing social interactions can be effectively represented through value decomposition into individual value maps for each agent and low-dimensional interaction terms. We develop a multi-agent inverse reinforcement learning framework (MAIRL) to infer these representations from behavior. In mouse and primate social tasks, MAIRL reveals interpretable value maps that are conditioned on the distinct social roles animals play during group behavior. Together, these results establish MAIRL as an interpretable and scalable framework for identifying latent value representations guiding multi-agent behavior across species. 1

# 1 Introduction 1

Social interactions are central to survival and daily life [ 1– 5], yet deciphering the computations that govern 2

them remains a fundamental challenge. In these settings, one’s decisions both influence and depend on 3

the actions of others, making it difficult to determine how the brain evaluates outcomes and integrates 4

social information. Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) is a powerful approach for studying single-agent 5

behavior by inferring its latent value functions that encode an agent’s goals [ 6 –9]. This framework has 6

been instrumental in linking behavior to neural representations [10, 11] and in understanding how these 7

computations break down in neuropsychiatric disorders [ 12 , 13 ]. Extending IRL to social interactions 8

is therefore essential for uncovering the computational principles underlying social cognition and its 9

dysfunction. 10 

However, applying this framework to social interactions introduces key challenges: the latent value 11 

functions are defined over the joint state space of interacting agents, which grows exponentially with the 12 

number of agents. To manage this complexity, many existing cognitive and neuroscience models rely on 13 

strong structural simplifications, limiting their applicability across diverse social tasks. One common 14 

strategy embeds social influences as latent variables within a single-agent Partially Observed Markov 15 

Decision Process (POMDP) framework [ 14 – 17 ]. While effective for capturing behavior, these approaches 16 

obscure the structure of social interactions by collapsing them into unobserved latent factors. Other 17 

approaches [ 18 , 19 ] adopted multi-agent Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) to infer simplified, often 18 

binary, social intentions to improve task performance, but do not recover the full value structure. Related 19 

work [ 20 ] assumes distinct self-value functions across cognitive hierarchies, conflating task-related value 20 

structure with agents’ recursive reasoning. These limitations highlight the need for a framework that 21 

can recover the complete and interpretable value functions directly from multi-agent behavior. This 22 

would enable direct inference of how agents represent both the environment and their social partners, 23 

disentangling task structure from social reasoning and allowing quantitative comparison of strategies. 24 

To enable inverse inference of latent value functions from behavior, we first construct a generative 25 

forward model that links value functions to observable social decisions under well-defined computational 26 

assumptions. We formalize social tasks within a multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) framework 27 

[ 21 ], which specifies how multiple agents act and interact in a shared environment. We show that joint 28 

value functions governing social interactions can be efficiently represented through value decomposition, 29 

which factorizes them into individual value components for each agent and low-dimensional interaction 30 

terms. This representation reduces the dimensionality of the inference problem while preserving task-31 

relevant interactions between agents, enabling tractable and interpretable recovery of joint value functions 32 

from behavior. We show analytically and numerically that, in typical social tasks where joint rewards are 33 

sparse, this decomposition accurately reconstructs the full joint value function. Within this framework, 34 

task structure is encoded in the joint value function, while agents’ cognitive hierarchy is captured by 35 

recursive reasoning [22], with recursion depth corresponding to theory-of-mind capacity. 36 

Building on this formulation, we develop a multi-agent inverse reinforcement learning (MAIRL) 37 

framework [ 6, 7 , 23 ] that infers decomposed value representations from observed behavior, thereby 38 

recovering agents’ internal representations of both the environment and their social partners. This 39 

inference algorithm accurately recovers the ground-truth value functions in simulated social foraging 40 

tasks, validating the approach under controlled conditions. We then apply MAIRL to empirical data 41 

from mouse cooperative behavior, revealing how asymmetric value learning across agents gives rise to 42 

leadership and followership. In a non-cooperative primate task, MAIRL uncovers mutual prediction 43 

signals correlated with social hierarchy. Together, these results demonstrate that value decomposition 44 

through MAIRL enables interpretable, scalable inference of the goals and strategies underlying social 45 

interactions across tasks and species. 46

2.3 Validation of MAIRL in simulated cooperative foraging with heterogeneous strategies 116 

Having established the accuracy of the OLS-based reconstruction, we next asked whether MAIRL can 117 

infer decomposed value functions from simulated trajectories of agents with distinct reasoning strategies 118 

in the cooperative match task (Fig. 2A). Under independent action selection, the model should recover 119 

each agent’s value function separately when the agents follow different decision strategies. Following 120 

Section 4.4, we simulated two agents with different reasoning policies: an egocentric, theory-of-mind 121 

level-0 (ToM-0) agent, which navigates to reward locations without considering its partner’s behavior, 122 

and a socially aware, theory-of-mind level-1 (ToM-1) agent, which assumes that its partner behaves 123 

as a ToM-0 agent and plans accordingly. Example simulated trajectories are shown in Fig. 2B and C. 124 

From behavioral observations alone, including trajectory length and port choice, these two strategies are 125 

difficult to distinguish. 126 

We then applied MAIRL separately to each agent’s trajectories to assess model fit and identify the 127 

value components underlying their distinct strategies. First, behavioral fitting using the decomposed 128 

value function achieved consistently higher log-likelihood (LL) than the full joint value function across 129 

ten random initializations (Fig. 2D). This improvement is likely due to the reduced parameterization of 130 

the decomposed model, which makes the optimization less susceptible to local minima. Second, model 131 

comparison allowed us to assess which terms in Eq. 5 are necessary for capturing and distinguishing 132 

the agents’ underlying foraging strategies. The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) results of fitting 133 

the trajectories from both agents are shown in Fig. 2E and F. A BIC drop greater than 10 is generally 134 

considered strong evidence in favor of the lower-BIC model. For the socially aware agent (Fig. 2E), 135 

models composed of both self position and partner distance were preferred, whereas for the egocentric 136

2.4 Applications to experimental datasets 143 

After validating our framework in simulations where ground truth is available, we applied the approach 144 

to the observed trajectories of two mice performing a cooperative foraging task [ 26 ] and tracked how 145 

their value representations evolved during learning. This analysis revealed value functions underlying 146 

distinct social roles and the unique dynamics in learning. Beyond cooperative settings, we further applied 147 

MAIRL to a non-cooperative "chicken" game in monkeys, demonstrating that the method generalizes 148 

across a range of social decision-making tasks. 149 

2.4.1 Infer value functions in cooperating mice with distinct social roles 150 

We first applied MAIRL to infer the value representations underlying a cooperative match task performed 151 

by a pair of mice (Fig.3A). Similar to the simulated task, the two mice must cooperate by navigating to 152 

the same reward zones to obtain rewards [ 26 ]. The arena (45cm → 45cm) was discretized into a 10 → 10 153 

grid, and animal movements were mapped onto this grid with nine possible actions (stay, up, down, left, 154 

right, and four diagonal movements). Example trajectory pairs are shown in Fig. 3B and C, where the 155 

animals selected the goal location in the east and coordinated their arrival. 156 

Mice adopt an emergent leader–follower strategy [ 26 ] to solve this task: the leader arrives first at the 157 

reward zone in most of the trials and has a disproportionate impact on follower’s choice. Using MAIRL, 158 

we sought to infer the value functions underlying each animal’s decisions and to determine how these 159 

values relate to their social roles. To this end, we constructed a set of nested models to test whether each 160 

component of the decomposed value function was necessary for explaining the behavior. We analyzed 161 

data from 27 well-trained animal pairs (Fig.3E-H). For most animals, models incorporating self-location 162 

and partner distance provided the best fit, whereas the partner’s allocentric location contributed minimally. 163 

Notably, partner distance improved model fit more for followers than for leaders. Overall, the best-fitting 164 

models significantly outperformed chance-level baselines (Fig. 3F) and accurately predicted final goal 165 

choices (Fig. 3G). 166 

We then directly visualized the value maps inferred from observed trajectories during cooperative task. 167 

Both leaders and followers (Fig. 4C, middle) assigned higher value to the goal locations. By contrast, 168 

followers assigned significantly greater value on proximal partner distance compared to leaders (Fig. 4C, 169 

right). Together, these results suggest that animals learn that successful coordination in this task depends 170 

primarily on maintaining appropriate mutual distance, with followers relying more heavily on partner 171 

distance compared to leaders. 172 

To determine whether MAIRL reliably reveals decision strategies across different behavioral contexts, 173 

we compared cooperative foraging to free-run sessions, when animals explore the arena out of the task 174 

context while still water-restricted (Fig. 4D). Model comparison (Fig. 4E) indicates that both leaders and 175 

followers were best described by models in which decisions depended only on self-location. Although 176 

animals continued to assign elevated value to the goal locations (Fig.4F, middle)—likely because these 177 

sessions were usually recorded right before task sessions and the well-trained animals continued to visit 178 

goal locations in anticipation of potential reward—they no longer incorporated partner distance into their 179 

value functions (Fig. 4F, right). This suggests that higher valuation of partner proximity is selective 180 

during the cooperative task and absent when animals are not cued to coordinate. 181 

Taken together, these results suggest that successful coordination in this task depends critically on 182 

the value representation of partner distance, with followers relying more on partner proximity than 183 

leaders. Importantly, these value maps are latent and not directly accessible from behavioral observations 184

2.4.2 Reveal the dynamics of value functions during learning 187 

We next applied MAIRL to track value functions across learning, enabling a closer examination of the 188 

dynamics in value assignment. The behavioral trajectories from the same 27 animal pairs were used. 189 

Because the value functions were estimated for each training session, we increased the discretization bin 190 

size and estimated values on a coarser 5 → 5 grid. 191 

Across learning, value functions for self-location remained largely stable (Fig. 5A and B), likely 192 

reflecting prior training and consistent knowledge of the goal locations. In contrast, the value function for 193 

partner distance progressively increased at close proximity over training (Fig. 5C), while the corresponding 194 

representation in leaders showed little change (Fig. 5D). These results suggest that followers undergo 195 

greater value updating during cooperative learning, selectively reinforcing proximity to their partners. 196 

To quantify this effect at the population level, we pooled the values assigned to proximal partner 197 

distance across all 27 animal pairs over learning (Fig. 5E and F). During early learning, followers rapidly 198 

increased the value of proximal partner distance, contributing to the increase in coordination success, 199 

whereas leaders showed slower and more limited adjustments. Consistent with this pattern, values for 200 

proximal partner distance were indistinguishable between followers and leaders at the onset of training 201 

(Fig.5G) but became significantly higher in followers after training (Fig. 5H). Together, these results 202 

reveal distinct learning strategies for followers and leaders, suggesting that role differentiation emerges 203 

through differences in value updating. Importantly, MAIRL reveals these latent value representations 204 

that are not directly observable from the behavior, providing a window into the internal computations 205 

underlying cooperative learning. 206 

2.4.3 Probing additional behavioral structure via conditioning variables 207 

While the core MAIRL formulation captures social value structure through a compact decomposition, it 208 

also enables hypothesis-driven incorporation of additional behavioral details without explicitly expanding 209 

the state space. Rather than introducing new state dimensions, we treat candidate variables as conditional 210 

modulators of existing value components, allowing us to assess their contribution while preserving 211 

tractability. As a proof of concept, we examine whether relative head direction modulates the value 212 

associated with inter-animal distance. 213 

Relative head direction ( ϱ) was defined as the angle of the partner from the instantaneous heading of 214 

oneself. We model ϱ not as an independent state variable, but as a conditioning variable that modulates 215 

the distance-dependent value term: 216 

V (s | ϱ) = m(s1) + ϖ(d | ϱ). (2) We discretized the continuous angle ϱ into three categories—front, left, and right—and fitted these 217 

conditioned distance value maps to observed trajectories from the well-trained pairs (Fig. 6). Model 218 

comparison revealed that a subset of animals (6 followers and 5 leaders) showed significant improvement 219 

after introducing the additional parameters (Fig.3H). For these animals, conditioning on head direction 220 

reveals structured modulation of value for partner distance (Fig. 6). Notably, only when the value functions 221 

are conditioned on a frontal partner position, followers showed a stronger preference for proximal states 222 

than leaders (Fig. 6B), suggesting that followers are more likely to approach and reduce distance when 223 

their partner is positioned directly ahead. 224 

This analysis illustrates how MAIRL can flexibly test additional behavioral hypotheses without 225 

increasing the dimensionality of the state space. More broadly, it demonstrates that decomposed value 226 

representations provide a structured framework for incorporating and evaluating candidate variables, 227 

enabling more precise and interpretable characterization of social behavior while preserving computational 228 

tractability. 229

# 3 Discussion 252 

We developed a multi-agent inverse reinforcement learning (MAIRL) framework that enables tractable 253 

and interpretable inference of latent value representations in social decision-making. The key advance is 254 

a value decomposition formulation that represents joint value functions as structured combinations of 255 

individual components and low-dimensional interactions, allowing recovery of high-dimensional social 256 

value structure without enumerating the full joint state space. We show theoretically and numerically that 257 

this representation accurately reconstructs sparse joint value functions while greatly reducing parameter 258 

complexity. Building on this, MAIRL enables direct inference of agents’ strategies from behavior: in 259 

simulated cooperative tasks, it distinguishes policies with different levels of social reasoning through 260 

model comparison. Applying MAIRL to mouse cooperative behavior, we uncover interpretable value 261 

maps that reveal social role differentiation and distinct learning dynamics, with followers progressively 262 

increasing the value assigned to socially relevant states. Importantly, the interaction terms in the 263 

decomposition are user-defined, allowing hypothesis-driven specification of candidate social features and 264 

direct testing of their contributions to behavior. MAIRL further supports the incorporation of additional 265 

behavioral features, such as head direction, as conditional modulators of value representations without 266 

expanding the state space. Extending this framework to a non-cooperative “chicken” game in monkeys, 267 

we show that the inferred value functions and mutual predictions capture strategic behavior and relate to 268 

social hierarchy, illustrating the generality of the approach across species and social paradigms. 269 

The exponential growth of the joint state space with the number of agents is not only a challenge 270 

for computational modeling, but also a fundamental constraint for neural systems operating in social 271 

environments. In the social tasks studied here, only a small subset of variables, such as relative spatial 272 

position, are behaviorally relevant. In natural social interactions, however, agents must contend with many 273 

additional factors, including identity, history, and context, leading to a rapid increase in representational 274

The present formulation focuses on a minimal setting that isolates the core computational problem 283 

of inferring joint value structure from behavior. We assume full observability of the environment and 284

Extending the framework to partially observable settings would enable modeling how agents infer hidden 286 

states and beliefs during interaction. We further consider discrete state and action spaces without imposing 287 

a parametric form on the reward function, enabling flexible recovery of value structure. Extensions to 288 

continuous domains using appropriate function basis would allow scaling to more complex environments 289 

while preserving this interpretability. Finally, the tasks studied here primarily engage low levels of 290 

recursive reasoning; applying the framework to settings that require deeper strategic inference would 291 

provide a more complete characterization of cognitive hierarchy in social behavior. 292 

Together, these results establish MAIRL as a general framework for uncovering the computations 293 

underlying social interactions in biological and artificial systems. The framework naturally extends along 294 

several directions enabled by its decomposed structure. First, additional behavioral variables, such as 295 

motivation [ 29 ], movement kinetics [ 30 ], or vocal communication [ 31 ], can be incorporated to allow 296 

for systematic evaluation of their contribution to social behavior. Second, value decomposition enables 297 

scaling to larger groups by reducing parameter growth from exponential to approximately linear in the 298 

number of agents, making it possible to study structured interaction graphs and collective dynamics in 299 

larger social systems, such as schooling fish or social insects [ 5 , 32 , 33 ]. Third, applying MAIRL to 300 

tasks that require deeper strategic reasoning, such as the stag-hunt game [ 34 ], would enable quantitative 301 

inference of theory-of-mind capacities. These directions highlight that MAIRL provides a flexible and 302 

extensible foundation for investigating the computations governing complex social behavior. 303 

Lastly, with the rapid development of large language model (LLM) agents, the proposed framework 304 

may also provide a useful perspective for studying interactions in artificial multi-agent systems. Recent 305 

advances have enabled multiple LLM-based agents to communicate, negotiate, and coordinate in shared 306 

environments, giving rise to increasingly complex collective behaviors [ 35 ]. However, the mechanisms 307 

governing these interactions often remain difficult to interpret, as policies and internal representations are 308 

distributed across high-dimensional neural networks. By explicitly modeling value functions, beliefs, 309 

and recursive reasoning, the MAIRL framework offers a principled way to infer latent objectives and 310 

predictive structures from observed social behaviors. This approach could help reveal how cooperative 311 

or competitive strategies emerge, how social roles differentiate within groups of agents, and how stable 312 

social conventions or equilibria arise over time. More broadly, applying such interpretable modeling 313 

tools to artificial agent societies may provide a quantitative bridge between studies of biological social 314 

cognition and the growing field of multi-agent artificial intelligence, enabling comparative analyses of 315 

learning dynamics, coordination strategies, and the emergence of collective behavior across natural and 316 

artificial systems. 317

4.4 Coordination and theory-of-mind (ToM) levels 372 

Another source of complexity in modeling multi-agent interactions arises from the need to account for 373 

coordination and recursive reasoning between agents. Each agent’s behavior depends not only on the 374 

environment but also on its beliefs about the strategies of others, introducing multiple levels of reasoning 375 

that must be modeled. For clarity, we focus on cooperative tasks in which agents share an identical value 376 

function. We further assume that each agent’s state is fully observable to the others. The theory-of-mind 377 

(ToM) levels considered in this work, corresponding to different depths of recursive reasoning, are 378 

summarized in Table 1. 379 

In the fully coordinated scenario, agents behave as if controlled by a centralized planner, executing 380 

the optimal joint action defined by Eq. 6. In the following, ε denotes this optimal joint policy, which 381 

may differ from the policies actually implemented by individual agents. 382 

In a less coordinated scenario, agents independently execute their policy, which is often the case 383 

in many simultaneous action games. Mathematically, we have conditional independence of individual 384 

policies P (a|s) = P (a1|s)P (a2|s). The question then becomes how each agent chooses its own action 385 

based solely on state information, without knowing the other agents’ action plans. The answer can be 386 

formulated through a probabilistic view of recursive reasoning, as described below: 387

4.6 Simulation of the cooperative foraging task 442 

For the simulated trajectories shown in Fig. 2, the arena was discretized into a 5-by-5 grid with determin-443 

istic action transitions, resulting in 625 states and 25 actions in the two-agent MDP. Two terminal states 444 

are defined by the simultaneous presence of both agents at the same reward location either in the north or 445 

east. If an agent reaches the boundary of the arena, its position is reset to that of the previous time step. 446 

The reward strength at both goal locations was set to 2, as absolute reward magnitude does not affect 447 

the inferred value structure. At the beginning of each trial, both agents are placed randomly in the arena. 448 

Agents are assumed to act under independent control. Agent 2 follows an egocentric (ToM level-0) policy, 449 

whereas agent 1 is modeled as a ToM level-1 agent that predicts agent 2’s behavior. In all simulations, 450 

500 trials were generated, each with a maximum length of 200 steps. The discount factor ω was set to 0.9. 451

4.7 Pre-processing of mouse foraging trajectories 452 

For inference using observed mouse foraging trajectories, each mouse was represented as a massless 453 

point anchored at its neck location. The arena was divided into a 10 !10 grid with 5cm spatial bins (10cm 454 

bins in Fig. 5 and 6). An animal’s grid position was determined by assigning its actual location to the 455 

nearest grid point. To minimize artifacts introduced by discretizing the arena, we excluded time steps 456 

where both animals remained stationary. Each animal had nine possible actions: stay, up, down, left, right, 457 

up-left, up-right, down-left, and down-right. The objective was to estimate a value map that, given both 458 

animals’ current location, maximizes the probability of the observed action. The partner angle in Fig. 6 459 

was defined as the angle of partner’s position relative to the agent’s own head direction. This angle was 460 

discretized into three bins: front (-60 to 60 degree), left (60 to 180 degree) and right (180 to 300 degree), 461 

such that ϱ ↑ {0, 1, 2}. The discretized angle was included as an input to the model. To future limit 462 

model complexity, the decomposed joint value function incorporates angle only through the interaction 463 

term. 464
